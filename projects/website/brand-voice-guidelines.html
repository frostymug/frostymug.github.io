Aspen Frost Brand Voice Guidelines

Owner: Zev (Marketing Lead) Status: Working guideline, subordinate to the approved Positioning Brief Created: 2026-06-23 Sources: Approved Positioning Brief v1.4; Andy voice notes; Reddit/r/ClaudeAI post on AI-writing tells, 2026-06-22/23: https://www.reddit.com/r/ClaudeAI/s/3LRpdd604K

These notes translate the approved positioning into day-to-day writing rules for website copy, agent descriptions, blog drafts, and public-facing Aspen Frost language.

---

The Voice

Aspen Frost should sound like one very specific human building something real with a strange, competent team around him.

The register is:

  • Serious about the craft.
  • Humble and open about the process.
  • Genuinely enjoying the build.
  • Plain enough to be trusted.
  • Specific enough that it could not belong to any other company.

The copy should feel said, not arranged.

---

The Core Rule

Say the thing directly. When the sentence has landed, stop.

Bad Aspen Frost copy usually does one of three things:

  • It adds a clever second clause after the point is already made.
  • It reaches for a polished abstraction when a concrete noun would do.
  • It explains the brand mood instead of showing the work.

Good Aspen Frost copy uses real nouns, real verbs, real stakes, and real proof.

Example principle: Frost Fitness is live beats a real product in real use unless the broader category matters in that sentence.

---

AI-Scent Checks

Andy flagged the Reddit post on AI-writing tells as especially useful. The important lesson is not a banned-word list. The bigger tell is polished copy with uniform rhythm, agreeable posture, and no claim inside it.

Watch for these patterns:

  • The antithesis crutch: not just X, it's Y; more than X, it's Y; X isn't only Y, it's Z.
  • Mirrored rhythm: neat clauses that echo each other because they sound tidy, not because they clarify the idea.
  • Five-paragraph gravity: intro, three tidy points, conclusion, even when the thought only needed three sentences.
  • Relentless positivity: everything is exciting, powerful, thoughtful, meaningful, transformative.
  • Fluent emptiness: the paragraph scans well but makes no testable claim.
  • Assistant residue: great question, hope this helps, it's important to note, let's dive in, or throat-clearing before the real answer.
  • Deck diction: leverage, seamless, delve, landscape, transform, unlock, elevate, robust, comprehensive when a plainer word would carry more trust.

Use the test Andy already likes: read it out loud. If the rhythm sounds like a model trying to be a good student, cut it down.

---

What To Do Instead

  • Start with the claim, not the setup.
  • Let sentence length vary naturally.
  • Use contractions where Andy would use them.
  • Prefer one strong sentence to a balanced pair.
  • Name the concrete thing: Frost Fitness, OpenClaw, the agent, the pull request, the build, the customer.
  • Take a position. Do not sand every edge into neutrality.
  • Leave some oxygen. A page does not need to prove every thought in every paragraph.

Revision move: after drafting, delete the first sentence and the last sentence of any paragraph that smells too polished. The real point is often in the middle.

---

Pronouns

Use I for Andy as narrator, owner, origin, taste, and intent.

Use we for the system at work: agents, process, culture, pipeline, projects, and delivery.

Pattern: I made the system. We use the system. We build your project.

Corporate we should never hide the interesting truth: Aspen Frost is one person and a system of agents he built to work together.

---

Anti-Brand

Avoid:

  • Buzzword soup.
  • Too many mentions of AI.
  • Copy that could belong to any agentic consultancy.
  • Cute jokes about the software.
  • Over-explaining the premise.
  • Claims that make the company sound more mature than it is.
  • Polished language that hides uncertainty instead of naming it honestly.

The charm is the true setup played straight.

---

Standing Review Habit

Every meaningful public draft gets one pass for AI scent:

  • Does this make a claim?
  • Could Andy say it out loud without wincing?
  • Is there a not just X, it's Y shape hiding in here?
  • Did we use a concrete noun where a polished abstraction would normally go?
  • Is the rhythm varied, or does it march?

If the answer is fuzzy, simplify the sentence before polishing it.
