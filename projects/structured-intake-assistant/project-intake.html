Structured Intake Assistant - Project Intake

> Status: Draft > Owner: Andy + Aspen > Date opened: 2026-06-24 > Current gate: Intake / Safety-Gated > Source: Erik screenshot and SCID PDF forwarded by Andy via Telegram on 2026-06-24.

1. Overview

Explore whether Aspen Frost should build a tool that helps administer, streamline, or operationalize a highly structured psychiatric intake/interview workflow inspired by the SCID, while staying inside safe clinical, legal, privacy, and licensing boundaries.

2. Background

Erik texted Andy with what he called a poorly thought out idea for an Aspen Frost project. He described the SCID, or Structured Clinical Interview for DSM-5, as a research-oriented, highly structured interview intended to assess broadly across diagnoses. He said his clinical assistant AI at work keeps suggesting that they do one, but that it is not practical because it takes at least an hour to administer.

Erik asked whether Jesse could build something that could administer it and said he has a 98-page PDF version. Andy asked Aspen to spin up a project and then sent the referenced PDF. Additional Andy/Erik back-and-forth is still expected.

3. Motivation

The practical pain is that comprehensive structured assessment may be clinically useful but too time-consuming for normal use. The possible opportunity is to reduce friction, increase consistency, or create a clinician-supervised workflow that captures structured information without making the interview impossible to administer.

Aspen Frost's motivation is exploratory only at this stage. This project could be valuable, but it also sits in a sensitive clinical domain where false confidence would be dangerous.

4. Goals

  • Understand Erik's actual workflow and pain point.
  • Determine whether a safe, bounded, clinician-supervised tool concept exists.
  • Identify licensing/IP constraints around the source interview and PDF.
  • Declare the correct stakes-ramp level before any design or implementation work.
  • Decide whether this should proceed, pause, or become a narrow research spike.

5. Objectives

  • Capture the PDF and chat context from Andy/Erik without reproducing licensed clinical content into project docs.
  • Produce an intake recommendation after Petra and Arya review the source material.
  • Define a non-diagnostic MVP candidate only if one is safe and useful.
  • Identify whether real patient data, PHI, or regulated clinical workflow would be involved.

6. Non-Goals

  • Building an autonomous diagnostic system.
  • Replacing clinician judgment.
  • Making DSM diagnoses or treatment recommendations.
  • Storing or processing real patient data during early discovery.
  • Reproducing proprietary SCID materials without permission.
  • Shipping anything into a live care workflow without explicit safety, privacy, clinical, and reliability review.

7. Current State

Known current state from the screenshot and received PDF:

  • Erik knows of the SCID and has a 98-page PDF version.
  • Andy forwarded the referenced PDF on 2026-06-24; it is preserved as sources/2026-06-24-scid-5-cv.pdf. The file appears to be a 98-page scanned/image PDF.
  • A clinical assistant AI at Erik's work suggests administering it.
  • The barrier is administration time: at least one hour.
  • Erik's ask is initially framed as something Jesse could build to administer it.

Unknowns:

  • Who would use the tool.
  • Whether patients or clinicians interact with it.
  • Whether this is for research, screening, clinical intake, supervision, documentation, or education.
  • Where the PDF came from and whether it is licensed for reuse or software encoding.
  • Whether any PHI would be used.
  • Whether Erik's workplace can approve such a tool.

8. Stakeholders & Users

  • Andy: Aspen Frost Visionary/CPO and intake gate.
  • Aspen: Integrator and project record owner.
  • Erik Frost: External stakeholder and domain source.
  • Potential clinicians: Possible users or reviewers, depending on workflow.
  • Potential patients/clients: Possibly affected users if the project enters clinical workflow; not in scope until safety posture is explicit.
  • Erik's workplace: Possible operational/compliance stakeholder if this is connected to work.

9. Petra - Customer, Product, and Market Requirements

Analysis

  • Target users / customer segments: Unknown. Candidate users include clinicians, intake coordinators, researchers, or Erik's internal clinical assistant AI context.
  • Jobs to be done: Make structured psychiatric assessment more practical without losing clinical rigor.
  • Current alternatives: Full SCID administration, unstructured clinical interviews, existing EHR/intake forms, clinician notes, and clinical-assistant-AI suggestions.
  • Differentiation or strategic edge: Unknown until Erik clarifies workflow and constraints.
  • Market/category context: Clinical mental-health tooling; likely regulated and trust-heavy.
  • Product requirements: Must avoid autonomous diagnosis and must support clinician oversight if it proceeds.

Questions

  • What exact problem does Erik want solved: time, consistency, documentation, triage, research data quality, or clinician adherence?
  • Who is the buyer/owner versus the user?
  • Is this for Curate Mental Health, Erik's workplace, or a private exploratory idea?
  • What would make Erik say this is useful without needing it to diagnose anyone?

Recommendation

Research. Do not proceed to design or build until source materials and workflow context are captured.

10. Izzy - Design Questions and Experience Risks

Analysis

  • Primary user journeys: Unknown. Could be clinician-administered, patient-facing pre-interview, AI-guided interview, or documentation assistant.
  • UX/design risks: A patient-facing clinical interview can feel alienating, leading, overly long, or falsely authoritative.
  • Accessibility considerations: Mental-health workflows require careful language, pacing, distress handling, and clear clinician escalation.
  • Learning/onboarding needs: Clinicians need to understand what the tool does and does not claim.
  • Interaction or information architecture questions: The structure of the interview may be complex and branching.

Questions

  • Who answers the questions, and in what setting?
  • What happens when answers indicate risk, distress, suicidality, psychosis, substance concerns, or trauma?
  • What should the tool do when a user does not understand or refuses a question?
  • What does a clinician review screen need to show to remain trustworthy?

Recommendation

Research. Design should wait for workflow boundaries and safety escalation rules.

11. Arya - Initial Architecture Thoughts

Analysis

  • Existing technical context: None yet beyond a referenced PDF.
  • Plausible architecture options: Static prototype, local-only clinician demo, LLM-assisted structured interviewer, rule-based branching form, or EHR-integrated workflow. These should not be chosen before risk review.
  • Data model / data source concerns: DSM-linked interview structure, branching logic, responses, clinician notes, audit trail, and PHI classification.
  • Integration risks: EHR, workplace AI, secure storage, authentication, compliance, and PDF/licensing constraints.
  • Build-vs-buy considerations: There may be existing licensed instruments, clinical intake systems, or research tools.
  • Unknowns requiring review: SCID license terms, PDF provenance, intended deployment environment, data retention, and human review workflow.

Questions

  • Can SCID content legally be encoded into software by Aspen Frost?
  • Is the desired tool deterministic branching, LLM conversation, transcript summarization, or workflow documentation?
  • What data is stored and where?
  • What must be auditable?
  • What model/provider constraints apply if PHI is involved?

Recommendation

Research / risk review. Arya should perform an early architecture and IP/data-risk pass before Jesse touches implementation.

12. Zev - Positioning and Communication

Analysis

  • Product promise: Must be narrow and careful. Possible framing: structured intake workflow assistant, not diagnostic AI.
  • Audience trust requirements: Very high. Clinicians, patients, and organizations must trust limits, privacy, and review paths.
  • Naming/brand/story considerations: Avoid naming that implies SCID ownership or diagnostic authority.
  • Launch or stakeholder communication risks: Over-claiming could create legal, clinical, and reputational risk.

Questions

  • What promise can Aspen Frost honestly make here?
  • Should the public/project-facing name avoid any SCID reference entirely?
  • What language keeps Erik's excitement while making the safety posture clear?

Recommendation

Research. Hold external positioning until scope is known.

13. Cassian - Reliability and Operations

Analysis

  • Deployment surface: Unknown.
  • Monitoring/health needs: If clinical workflow enters scope, uptime, audit trails, backups, and incident response matter.
  • Failure modes: Wrong branch, missed risk signal, hallucinated interpretation, lost data, privacy leak, unavailable system, or unclear handoff to clinician.
  • Support and maintenance burden: Potentially high if used in practice.
  • Data backup/recovery needs: Critical if patient data is stored; not allowed in early shared-workspace discovery.

Questions

  • Will any PHI ever be processed?
  • What are the minimum security controls for a prototype?
  • Who supports it if it breaks?
  • What must be logged versus intentionally not retained?

Recommendation

Pause production thinking. Require privacy/security posture before any deployed prototype.

14. Stakes Ramp Declaration

Proposed Level

Level 5: Critical stakes for any tool that administers a diagnostic interview, processes PHI, affects diagnosis, or enters clinical workflow.

Potentially Level 4: High stakes only for a bounded, clinician-supervised, no-real-patient-data prototype or internal research spike.

Reasoning

  • The domain is psychiatry and DSM-linked clinical assessment.
  • The likely source material may be copyrighted/licensed.
  • If used with real patients, data is sensitive health information.
  • Incorrect behavior could influence diagnosis, treatment, or trust in care.

Highest-Risk Dimension

User harm if wrong, data sensitivity, privacy exposure, and regulated clinical workflow.

Mitigations

  • Keep early work to intake and research only.
  • Do not process real patient data in shared workspace tools.
  • Avoid autonomous diagnosis claims.
  • Require clinician supervision in any future concept.
  • Perform IP/licensing review before encoding source interview content.
  • Require Andy gate before design, architecture, or implementation.

Escalation Triggers

This remains Level 5 if any of the following are true:

  • Real patient/client data is used.
  • Output could affect diagnosis, treatment, triage, documentation, or care decisions.
  • The tool is deployed for Erik's workplace or Curate Mental Health.
  • The tool stores PHI or clinical records.
  • The tool uses LLMs to ask, interpret, score, summarize, or recommend without strict clinician review.
