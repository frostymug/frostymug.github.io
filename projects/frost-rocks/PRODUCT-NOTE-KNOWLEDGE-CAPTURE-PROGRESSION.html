Product Note — Knowledge Capture Progression

Petra | 2026-07-07

Prompt

Andy raised a second product/research question from the Frost Rocks learning-source conversation: what are the options, and possible progression, for capturing knowledge and learning?

The intuition: early Frost Rocks may only need lightweight methods appropriate for casual place-based learning. If the product later leans harder into general learning, tutoring, or education, we may need more robust learning methodologies.

Andy suggested this as a good Petra professional-development topic.

Product Question

How should Frost Rocks capture what the user is learning, and how should that approach mature as the product moves from casual experience-based learning toward more formal learning support?

Research Dimensions To Add At Each Level

As this progression is fleshed out, each level should be evaluated across three additional dimensions:

  • Review and consolidation: How does the product help the user revisit, retrieve, consolidate, and apply what they learned? FSRS is one concrete feature-level example, but the broader question includes memory consolidation, retrieval practice, spaced review, interleaving, and contextual resurfacing.
  • Academic grounding: Which findings from cognitive science, learning science, educational psychology, and adjacent research apply at this level? The goal is not academic theater; it is to know which claims are evidence-backed and which are product intuition.
  • Market practice: What are popular learning apps and adjacent products actually doing today? This should include direct learning apps and nearby categories such as language learning, flashcards, tutoring companions, note-taking/PKM tools, field guides, nature ID apps, and fitness-style habit systems.

This means each level should eventually answer:

  • What is captured?
  • How is it structured?
  • How is it reviewed?
  • What learning science supports it?
  • What app patterns already exist?
  • What user promise is strong enough to justify the complexity?

Working Progression

Level 1: Lightweight Reflection Capture

Best for: Finland prototype, casual learning, trip prep, field notes.

The user captures small moments in natural language:

  • "I listened to this."
  • "This made me think of..."
  • "This clicked because..."
  • "I saw this in the field."
  • "I want to remember this."

Primary value: preserve meaningful learning moments without turning the app into homework.

Risk: too loose to support strong review, assessment, or personalization.

Level 2: Tagged Learning Moments

Best for: early product once patterns emerge.

The app extracts or asks for lightweight structure:

  • Source.
  • Topic.
  • Place/context.
  • User takeaway.
  • Related prior knowledge.
  • Confidence or curiosity level.
  • Possible review prompt.

Primary value: makes learning moments retrievable and connectable.

Risk: if the capture flow becomes too structured, users stop doing it.

Level 3: Concept Graph / Understanding Graph

Best for: a maturing place-based learning app.

The app begins modeling relationships:

  • Concept A explains Concept B.
  • Example X helped the user understand Concept Y.
  • Place P is associated with Process Q.
  • Encounter E reinforced Source S.
  • User knows Topic T weakly/strongly/only by analogy.

Primary value: creates contextual resurfacing and personalized review.

Risk: the graph can become performative complexity unless it clearly improves learning outcomes.

Level 4: Evidence-Based Learner Model

Best for: when Frost Rocks cares more seriously about skill development.

The app distinguishes exposure from understanding using evidence:

  • User read/listened/watched.
  • User summarized.
  • User answered a retrieval prompt.
  • User recognized an example in the field.
  • User applied a concept to a new observation.
  • User explained a concept in their own words.

Primary value: avoids the false assumption that "seen" means "known."

Risk: begins to feel more educational/formal; may not fit all casual learners.

Level 5: Formal Learning Methodologies

Best for: future generalized learning/tutor companion or serious education product.

Possible methodologies to research:

  • Retrieval practice.
  • Spaced repetition / FSRS.
  • Concept mapping.
  • Knowledge tracing.
  • Mastery learning.
  • Bloom's taxonomy / cognitive skill levels.
  • Constructivist learning and prior-knowledge activation.
  • Metacognition and self-explanation.
  • Transfer of learning.
  • Assessment design and evidence-centered design.

Primary value: supports stronger claims about learning progress.

Risk: can overfit an informal, intrinsically motivated product to classroom assumptions.

Strategic Read

The right methodology depends on the product promise.

If the promise is "notice and remember the world more richly," lightweight reflection and contextual resurfacing may be enough.

If the promise is "get better at identifying rocks," we need retrieval practice, feedback, and evidence of applied understanding.

If the promise is "learn anything with a tutor companion," we need a much more serious learner model and assessment strategy.

Decision Principle

Match learning-method rigor to the user's intent and the product's claim.

Do not import educational machinery just because it is intellectually attractive. Use the minimum structure that makes the learning loop measurably better.

Professional Development Thread

Petra should do a research pass on methodologies for capturing and modeling learning across increasing levels of rigor:

  • Casual reflection and experience capture.
  • Informal lifelong learning.
  • Skill acquisition and retrieval practice.
  • Learner modeling and knowledge tracing.
  • Formal educational assessment.

Output should be a practical product brief, not an academic survey.

The brief should examine each level through three lenses:

  • Review and consolidation, including retrieval practice, spaced repetition, FSRS, contextual resurfacing, interleaving, and memory consolidation.
  • Academic research and findings from cognitive science, learning science, educational psychology, and related fields.
  • Current market practices in popular apps, including language learning, flashcards, tutoring companions, note-taking/PKM tools, field guides, nature ID apps, and habit-oriented products.

Strategic Lean

For Frost Rocks now: start at Level 1 or Level 2.

For Frost Rocks later: consider Level 3 and Level 4 if the product demonstrates that connected learning and skill development are core to retention.

For a generalized learning companion: only consider Level 5 after we have evidence that users want the product to make stronger learning claims.
