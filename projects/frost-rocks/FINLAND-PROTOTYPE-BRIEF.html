Frost Rocks — Finland Prototype Brief

Petra | June 2026

Why This Prototype

Andy is going to Finland in about a month. That creates a useful constraint: build something small enough to use on a real trip, but rich enough to test whether Frost Rocks is actually about casual adult learning, memory, and place rather than merely rock identification.

This should be treated as a user-zero field prototype, not an MVP. The goal is not to prove the whole business. The goal is to learn whether a travel-linked geology/natural-history journal creates enough personal value that Andy wants to keep using it after the trip.

The existing Frost Rocks strategy still holds: the opportunity is off-field skill development, not competing directly with dominant AI rock ID apps. The Finland prototype should make the in-field moment lightweight and the at-home review meaningful.

Product Hypothesis

Older curious adults in a life-transition stage want more than answers. They want a record of what they noticed, where they were, what they learned, and how their understanding changed over time. A lightweight app that turns travel encounters into later reflection and practice may help them build durable knowledge without making the trip feel like homework.

That is an interesting hypothesis. How might we test it? With Andy in Finland, using the product in the messy real conditions where the idea either has oxygen or does not.

Target User For This Prototype

Primary user: Andy as user zero.

Proxy segment:

  • Empty nester or near-retirement adult learner
  • Curious, educated, and self-directed
  • Interested in geology, natural history, landscape, and travel context
  • Wants to remember and understand places, not just collect facts
  • Comfortable with phone capture, but does not want the phone to dominate the outdoor experience

The emotional job is important: "Help me turn this trip into something I keep learning from."

Prototype Learning Goals

The prototype should answer five questions.

  • Does Andy actually log encounters while traveling, or does capture feel like friction?
  • Are place and event context motivating enough to make later review feel personal rather than academic?
  • Does a lightweight learning record increase the feeling of "I am understanding Finland better"?
  • What kind of content is useful in the moment: rock ID, geological story, natural-history context, prompts, or all of these?
  • Does Andy review after returning home without being nagged into it?

Finland Content Frame

Finland is a strong test location because the landscape has an obvious geological story: ancient bedrock, glaciation, lakes, forests, islands, and human settlement patterns shaped by ice and stone. The prototype does not need a comprehensive Finland geology database. It needs enough content scaffolding to make Andy notice better.

Suggested content themes:

  • Ancient Fennoscandian Shield bedrock
  • Granite and gneiss as common visual anchors
  • Glacial shaping: scraped bedrock, erratics, eskers, moraines, lake basins
  • Finnish lake and forest ecology as natural-history context
  • Shorelines, islands, and exposed rock as recurring observation sites
  • Built environment: stone in churches, streets, walls, memorials, and public spaces

The product should not pretend it can perfectly identify every rock from a travel photo. It should be honest: "Here is what you might look for," "Here is what this place may reveal," and "Here is what remains uncertain." Trust matters more than swagger.

Instructional Design

The existing Frost Rocks design principles still apply, but the Finland prototype should use them gently.

Encounter First

The user captures a moment before receiving content. A logged encounter can include:

  • Photo or photos
  • Location
  • Date and time
  • Optional event label, such as "Helsinki walk," "National park hike," or "ferry day"
  • Freeform note
  • Initial guess or observation
  • Confidence: Sure / Kind of / Guessing

The key is not "What is this rock?" The better prompt is "What did you notice?" That preserves the learning posture.

Scaffolding After Attempt

After Andy makes an observation or guess, the app can offer help:

  • "Look again for texture, layering, crystal size, color variation, or relation to the surrounding landscape."
  • "Could this be bedrock, a transported glacial erratic, or a human-placed stone?"
  • "What would distinguish granite from gneiss in this photo?"

This keeps the attempt-first principle without making the trip feel like a quiz.

Memory-Based Review

After the trip, encounters become review cards. Cards should feel like memories, not school drills.

Example review prompts:

  • "Where were you when you saw this?"
  • "What did you think this was at the time?"
  • "What feature would you look for now?"
  • "What does this place tell you about Finland's geological history?"
  • "What are you still unsure about?"

The product can use spaced review later, but the prototype does not need a full FSRS implementation if that slows learning. A simple "Review soon / Review later / I know this now" loop is enough for the travel test.

UX Concept

The prototype should have four primary surfaces.

1. Trip Home

Purpose: orient Andy around the current Finland trip.

Core elements:

  • Trip title: Finland 2026
  • Map/list toggle of logged encounters
  • Today's captures
  • Light prompt of the day, for example "Notice where exposed stone appears: natural surface, building material, shoreline, path, or monument."
  • Review queue count, kept quiet during the trip

This should feel like a field notebook, not a dashboard.

2. Capture Encounter

Purpose: log quickly without killing the moment.

Minimum fields:

  • Add photo
  • Auto location and timestamp
  • "What did you notice?" note field
  • Optional initial guess
  • Confidence: Sure / Kind of / Guessing
  • Event tag

Nice-to-have fields:

  • Mood or memory tag
  • "Ask me about this later" toggle
  • Offline save indicator

The capture flow must be usable in under 30 seconds. If it is not, we are designing for our concept instead of Andy's vacation.

3. Encounter Detail

Purpose: connect a logged moment to learning.

Core elements:

  • Photo gallery
  • Map/location context
  • Andy's original note and guess
  • Learning note generated or curated from Finland content
  • Observation prompts
  • "What I learned later" note field
  • Uncertainty marker: identified / likely / unknown

Important design stance: uncertainty should be first-class. A thoughtful unknown is better than a fake answer.

4. Review At Home

Purpose: turn trip memories into durable knowledge.

Core elements:

  • Daily review stack of selected encounters
  • Memory-first prompt before answer/context reveal
  • Confidence check
  • Small "what changed?" reflection
  • Progress view by theme: glacial landforms, bedrock, shorelines, built stone, ecology

Review should be quiet and satisfying. No streak confetti. This audience wants progress, not carnival behavior.

Prototype Scope

Must Have

  • Create a Finland trip
  • Add photo encounters
  • Save location, timestamp, note, event tag, and confidence
  • View encounters as list and map
  • Add or edit learning notes
  • Mark uncertainty level
  • Review saved encounters after the trip
  • Work with weak or no connectivity while capturing

Should Have

  • Prompt of the day
  • Curated Finland geology/natural-history starter pack
  • Simple review scheduling
  • Exportable trip journal
  • Basic analytics on usage and review behavior

Could Have

  • AI-assisted draft learning note after Andy has made an observation
  • Rock Identifier or web lookup link-out
  • "Compare these two encounters" review card
  • Photo annotation for visible features

Do Not Build Yet

  • Full AI rock identification
  • Public community
  • Complete rock database
  • Social feed
  • Gamified streak system
  • Heavy onboarding curriculum

Content Pack Proposal

Build a small Finland starter pack before the trip. It should be curated, humble, and useful.

Recommended pack size:

  • 8 to 12 geology/natural-history themes
  • 20 to 30 short learning cards
  • 10 to 15 observation prompts
  • A small glossary for terms Andy is likely to encounter

Example cards:

  • "Why Finland has so many lakes"
  • "What glacial striations can tell you"
  • "Granite vs. gneiss: what to look for visually"
  • "What is an erratic?"
  • "How eskers form"
  • "Why exposed bedrock is common in parts of Finland"
  • "How forests, lakes, and bedrock shape Finnish landscapes together"

We should keep cards short. The trip is not a lecture hall. Dry Finnish bedrock deserves dignity, but not a 1,200-word essay in a phone UI.

UX Principles For Izzy

  • Treat the app as a field notebook with memory, not a quiz app with photos.
  • Make capture nearly invisible: photo, note, confidence, done.
  • Use place as the organizing principle. The map and trip timeline are the emotional architecture.
  • Let uncertainty look respectable.
  • Make review feel like returning to the trip, not being assigned homework.
  • Prefer quiet progress signals: themes noticed, memories reviewed, confidence improved.
  • Keep the interface mature, legible, and calm. The user is curious, not twelve.

What We Should Instrument

Even for a rough prototype, collect basic signals:

  • Number of encounters created per day
  • Time to complete capture
  • Percent with notes
  • Percent with event tags
  • Percent with initial guess or observation
  • Percent reviewed after capture
  • Review sessions after returning home
  • Edits to "what I learned later"
  • Encounters marked unknown versus likely identified

Qualitative diary prompts for Andy:

  • "When did this feel worth pulling out your phone?"
  • "When did it feel like friction?"
  • "What did you wish the app knew about where you were?"
  • "Which saved moments were you happiest to revisit?"
  • "Did this make you feel more connected to Finland, or just more organized?"

Suggested Prototype Build Shape

Fastest useful version:

  • Local-first mobile web app or simple native wrapper
  • SQLite or local JSON store with export
  • Map using saved GPS coordinates
  • Manual content pack loaded from Markdown/JSON
  • Optional AI only for drafting learning notes after user input

The prototype can be ugly in places, but it cannot lose data. For a travel prototype, reliability beats cleverness. Offline capture is not a luxury; it is the difference between field tool and decorative concept.

Review Questions For Andy And Izzy

  • Is this still Frost Rocks, or is it becoming a broader "landscape learning journal"?
  • Should the first screen be map, timeline, or capture?
  • How much explicit geology instruction belongs in the field versus at home?
  • What should uncertainty look like visually?
  • Is review organized by trip chronology, place, theme, or spaced-practice priority?
  • What emotional state should the app create: curiosity, mastery, nostalgia, or all three in sequence?

Petra Recommendation

Build the Finland prototype as a travel learning journal for geology and natural history, with rock identification treated as one learning mode inside a broader place-based experience.

That is a slight but important repositioning for the prototype. The durable product may still be Frost Rocks, but the test should not artificially narrow Andy's Finland experience to "what rock is this?" The stronger user value may be: "I noticed more, understood more, and came home with a living record of what I learned."

If that proves true, Frost Rocks has a more emotionally durable wedge than identification. If it proves false, we learn quickly and retreat to the tighter skill-development loop. Both outcomes are useful. Annoying, but useful.
