Design Note — Tutor Grounding & Anti-Hallucination

Author: Izzy (Design) · 2026-07-23 · Status: DRAFT for Andy — opened at Andy's direction after WoZ Pilot #3 Trigger: Pilot #3 finding F24 — the tutor confidently mis-IDd a rock feature (boudinage) that the learner correctly challenged as jointing, using a discrimination the tutor itself had taught. Related: VOICE-GUIDE-FIELD-DON-V1 (§6 uncertainty grammar), D3-WEDGE-SPEC (§5.1 prompt assembly, §4 model), AI-TUTOR-DESIGN-BRIEF (KM-1 challenge-able model; "authored vs improvised" meta).

---

1. The problem, stated precisely

The tutor makes factual claims about the physical world — mineral IDs, rock types, structural features, ages, mechanisms. Some are right; some are improvised and wrong. Tonight's boudinage call was the second kind: a plausible, exotic, confidently-delivered, incorrect identification, dressed in a warm authoritative register that makes it more believable.

The charm is a liability at exactly the moment the content is wrong. A tutor that hallucinates a confident wrong ID is worse than one that says "I can't tell from this photo," because it teaches a falsehood and trains misplaced trust in the source.

2. Two different "uncertainties" — do not conflate them

The voice guide already handles ONE of these. The gap is the other.

LayerWhat it isFailure modeStatus
Pedagogical uncertaintyTeaching epistemics: "likely / can't rule out / here's what would settle it." Models how a geologist reasons under incomplete evidence.Hedge soup, or false confidence as pedagogy.Canon (voice guide §6).
Factual groundingIs the claim actually true? Did the model see the feature correctly, or improvise a plausible label?Hallucination — fluent wrong ID.UNADDRESSED. This note.

These feel similar in the output ("I'd say likely gneiss…") but are different mechanisms. You can be perfectly calibrated in pedagogical grammar while being factually hallucinating — tonight's boudinage was phrased without enough hedging AND was wrong. Both layers failed at once.

3. Why this domain is high-risk for it

  • Vision IDs from a single amateur photo are genuinely hard — even a real geologist hedges heavily from one field photo (angle, scale, lighting, weathering, lichen). The model's confidence should almost never exceed a real expert's from the same photo.
  • Near-neighbors look alike — jointing vs. boudinage, foliation vs. banding vs. dike, granite vs. gneiss. The whole point of the app is these are confusable; the tutor is not magically immune to the confusion it teaches.
  • Exotic-explanation bias — the model reached for the rarer, more impressive term (boudinage) over the mundane correct one (jointing). A "sounds like a real geologist" pull toward vocabulary that impresses. This is a specific, nameable bias to guard against.
  • Authoritative register amplifies — the Field Don voice is designed to sound like a leader in the field. That trust is earned for pedagogy and weaponized for error.

4. Candidate mitigations (for discussion — not yet decisions)

Ordered rough cheapest→deepest. These are design directions; several need Jesse/Arya feasibility input.

  • Confidence-tiered claims with an honest floor. Every physical-world claim carries an internal confidence; low-confidence claims MUST surface as such ("from this one photo I genuinely can't tell X from Y — here's what would settle it") and MUST NOT be delivered in the same register as high-confidence ones. Ties factual confidence to the already-canon uncertainty grammar — but driven by actual claim confidence, not vibe.
  • Exotic-explanation guardrail. Explicit instruction: when a mundane and an exotic explanation both fit, PREFER the mundane and name the exotic as a possibility to check — never lead with the rare term. ("These read as joints; if they turn out to be stretched layers rather than open cracks, that'd be boudinage — worth a look.") This single rule would have caught tonight's error.
  • Photo-grounded claims only / "show the evidence." Require the tutor to tie each visual claim to a nameable feature in the image ("the dark lines are open gaps with shadow inside → cracks"). Forces the model to actually look, and makes claims falsifiable by the learner. Directly connects to F21 (photo-in-turn) — showing the crop IS grounding.
  • Learner-as-check is a feature, not a fallback (KM-1). The challenge-able model already anticipates this: the correct tutor behavior on challenge is to actually re-examine and concede (move 8), not defend. Tonight's correct recovery = re-look → "you're right, those are joints." Design the correction loop to be graceful and fast, and treat learner corrections as high-value model evidence.
  • Source/reference layer (Andy's original sidenote). Where claims can be tied to real references (regional geology, known formations — e.g. Wiborg rapakivi batholith, Svecofennian bedrock), ground them and optionally cite. Distinguish "general geological fact" (citable) from "ID of THIS rock from THIS photo" (inherently uncertain, never citable as fact). Feasibility: retrieval over a curated geology corpus vs. relying on model priors — open question for architecture.
  • Post-hoc verification for high-stakes claims. A second pass (self-critique or a cheaper verifier) on confident physical IDs before they land in ai_learning_note (persisted "truth"). Especially important because residue writes become the learner's record — a hallucination written to learned_later-adjacent fields becomes durable false memory.

5. The sharp line (connects to the brief's "authored vs improvised" meta)

The AI-Tutor brief already drew this for curriculum: the spine is authored/reviewed/versioned, not improvised per-session. Factual claims need the same discipline. A confident physical-world ID improvised at runtime with no grounding and no confidence floor is the factual-layer version of "freelancing the curriculum." The fix philosophy is consistent: improvisation is fine for phrasing and pedagogy; it is not fine for facts and curriculum.

6. Open questions for Andy / architecture

  • How much can/should we ground against a real geology corpus vs. trust model priors? (Retrieval feasibility → Jesse/Arya.)
  • Where's the confidence floor? What confidence is "too low to state as a lead"?
  • Do low-confidence IDs still get written to residue, and if so, flagged how? (Durable-false-memory risk.)
  • Is a verification second-pass worth the latency/cost per session? (D3-Q5 envelope.)
  • Does the learner-correction loop feed the tutor's calibration over time, not just the learner model?

---

Case study of record: Pilot #3, encounter e1dad4aa. Tutor said boudinage; learner said jointing; learner was right. The whole note exists so the shipped tutor loses that argument less often — and concedes gracefully when it does.
