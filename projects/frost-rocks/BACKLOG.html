P1 - Preserve native-quality photo metadata at capture/export

Status: Backlog - prominent candidate for next TestFlight follow-up / implementation planning Source: Andy in-field usage and Google Photos inspection, 2026-07-22 Area: Camera capture / photo persistence / encounter metadata Type: Capture-integrity gap in the core field-memory loop

Problem

Photos taken through Frost Rocks and later viewed in Google Photos appear to be missing expected metadata such as location, time zone context, camera/device details, and related capture information. Native iPhone Camera photos preserve much richer metadata by default, while the Frost Rocks camera pipeline may be saving image bytes without carrying forward or writing the full EXIF/TIFF/GPS metadata bundle.

For this product, metadata is not garnish. Location, time, device/camera context, and local trip context are part of what lets the app become a field memory system instead of a pile of pictures. If the app loses that context at capture, later review, map recall, export, AI assistance, and cross-encounter connection all get weaker.

User Story

As a field user, I want photos captured in Frost Rocks to preserve the same practical metadata I expect from native iPhone photos, so my encounters retain their place, time, and capture context when I review or export them later.

Why It Matters

  • Protects trust in the capture loop: the app should not silently downgrade the user's field record.
  • Strengthens the map, timeline, review, and future tutor surfaces with reliable place/time context.
  • Makes exported photos more useful outside Frost Rocks, including in Google Photos and downstream workflows.
  • Preserves the product thesis that the app helps users notice, remember, and connect observations over time.
  • Reduces future ambiguity when diagnosing whether missing location/time data is a user-permission issue, an image-pipeline issue, or an export issue.

Acceptance Criteria

  • Photos captured through Frost Rocks preserve available EXIF/TIFF metadata through local save and export paths.
  • GPS coordinates are attached to the photo file when location permission is granted and a location fix is available.
  • Capture timestamp is preserved in the image metadata and in the encounter record.
  • Local time zone is stored explicitly in the encounter record, even if it is not represented cleanly in standard EXIF fields.
  • Device/camera/lens metadata is preserved or written when available from the iOS capture pipeline.
  • Encounter database/export remains the canonical product metadata source; image EXIF is preserved for interoperability, not used as the only source of truth.
  • The app can distinguish "metadata unavailable because permission/data was missing" from "metadata lost during image processing."
  • Regression test/evidence: a Frost Rocks photo and a native iPhone Camera photo taken at the same place/time show comparable practical metadata in Apple Photos or Google Photos, allowing for fields iOS does not expose to third-party apps.

Product Notes

  • Treat this as capture integrity, not nice-to-have photo polish. A field notebook that loses place/time context is less trustworthy.
  • Avoid assuming EXIF alone solves the product problem. Store structured encounter metadata separately and intentionally, especially local time zone and app-specific trip/event context.
  • Be explicit about privacy: GPS metadata should depend on granted location permission and should be represented clearly in export behavior.
  • Likely technical risk: custom camera capture, image conversion, resizing, recompression, or upload/export paths may strip metadata unless explicitly preserved.

P1 - Export encounter data with linked photo files

Status: Backlog - prominent diagnostic/export candidate Source: Andy and Izzy export review workflow, 2026-07-22 Area: Export / diagnostics / research handoff Type: Diagnostic workflow gap in the field-learning loop

Problem

Andy and Izzy are reviewing Frost Rocks exports, but the current export requires them to stitch encounter data back together with the photos manually. The JSON contains encounter records and photo references, but the export does not behave like a self-contained review package where each encounter's data and visual evidence travel together.

That makes diagnostics slower and more error-prone. It also blocks the future workflow we clearly want: handing a complete encounter package to a human reviewer, designer, product strategist, or AI agent and asking, "what happened here, what did the user capture, and what should we learn from it?"

User Story

As someone reviewing field usage, I want an export package that includes encounter data and the corresponding photo files with stable links between them, so I can inspect the record without manually reassembling it.

Why It Matters

  • Speeds up Andy/Izzy/Petra/Arya review of real field sessions.
  • Reduces accidental mismatches between encounter records and photos.
  • Makes diagnostic handoffs practical for agents and humans.
  • Supports image-based review, metadata verification, and future AI assistance.
  • Turns export from a raw data dump into an evidence package.

Acceptance Criteria

  • Export can include both encounter data and the associated original/local photo files.
  • Each exported encounter has stable photo references that resolve within the export package.
  • Photo filenames are deterministic and include enough encounter/photo identity to avoid collisions.
  • Export includes a manifest that maps encounter IDs to photo files, capture timestamps, and key metadata availability.
  • Existing JSON-only export remains available if lightweight export is still useful.
  • The package can be opened outside the app without access to the app database or camera roll.
  • Export preserves photo metadata as implemented by the metadata-preservation backlog item; if metadata is missing, the manifest makes that visible.
  • Regression evidence: Andy/Izzy can review an exported session without manually matching image files to encounter records.

Product Notes

  • This is a diagnostic/research tool first, not a public sharing format. Do not overdesign presentation yet.
  • Prefer a plain package shape: manifest.json, encounters.json, and a photos/ directory or equivalent zip structure.
  • Keep original photo files available where possible. Derived thumbnails can be added later, but the diagnostic need is original evidence.
  • This pairs naturally with the metadata-preservation item above, but it should be tracked separately: a photo can be correctly linked and still have bad metadata, or preserve metadata while still being painful to export.

P1 - Edit note on an existing encounter

Status: Backlog - candidate for next TestFlight follow-up Source: Andy in-field usage, 2026-07-19 Area: Encounter detail / capture recovery Type: Usability gap in the core capture loop

Problem

Andy saved an encounter without taking a note, then immediately wanted to go back and add one quickly. The current app supports fast capture, but does not provide an obvious way to edit the user's note after the encounter already exists.

This is the predictable shadow of the camera-first flow: saving a photo-only encounter is good, but the app must make it easy to enrich that encounter later without making the user feel punished for moving quickly in the field.

User Story

As a field user, I want to open an existing encounter and edit or add my note, so I can capture observations after the moment without creating a duplicate encounter.

Why It Matters

  • Preserves the value of two-tap capture while closing the recovery loop.
  • Keeps the encounter record in Andy's words; AI enrichment must never overwrite this field.
  • Reduces duplicate or fragmented encounters created only because note editing is missing.
  • Supports the product thesis: a field notebook should be forgiving when used in the field.

Acceptance Criteria

  • From encounter detail, the user can enter an edit state for the personal note.
  • If the encounter has no note, the action reads as adding a note, not correcting a mistake.
  • Saving updates only the user's note field and updated_at; it must not overwrite ai_learning_note, photos, GPS, guess, confidence, event tag, or review state.
  • Cancel/back exits edit mode without changing the note.
  • Works offline and persists locally.
  • The edit path is reachable in two taps or fewer from the encounter detail screen.
  • Instrument encounter_note_edit_start, encounter_note_edit_save, and encounter_note_edit_cancel if the events table is present.

Product Notes

  • This should probably reuse the existing updateEncounter() path described in V2-IMPLEMENTATION-PLAN.md, but the UI should be narrower than the full enrich form: quick note edit first, broader encounter editing later only if usage proves the need.
  • Do not make this a generic "edit everything" feature yet. The observed pain is specifically "I forgot the note," not "I need a full CMS for encounters." We are staying disciplined. Shocking, I know.

P1 - Zoom into encounter images during review

Status: Backlog - candidate for next TestFlight follow-up Source: Andy in-field review-flow usage, 2026-07-19 Area: Review flow / image inspection Type: Usability gap in the core learning loop

Problem

Andy entered the review flow and wanted to zoom into the encounter image, but the current review surface does not support image zoom. That blocks close inspection of texture, grain, banding, crystal size, weathering, and other details that often determine what the rock is or what the user should notice.

Review is not just a memory prompt; it is the moment where the photo becomes field evidence. If the image cannot be inspected, the app weakens its own learning loop.

User Story

As a reviewing user, I want to pinch-zoom or tap into a larger view of an encounter image, so I can inspect geological details before answering or reflecting.

Why It Matters

  • Supports the discrimination-first learning thesis: users need to inspect diagnostic visual features.
  • Makes review useful for real rock ID, not just recognition of a thumbnail.
  • Reduces frustration when the relevant detail is visible in the photo but too small in the review UI.
  • Creates a reusable image-inspection pattern for encounter detail and future tutor/comparison surfaces.

Acceptance Criteria

  • In the review flow, the user can open or interact with the encounter image in a zoomable view.
  • Pinch-to-zoom works on device; double-tap zoom is a nice-to-have if cheap.
  • The zoomed image can be panned while zoomed.
  • The user can dismiss the zoom view and return to the same review card/state without losing progress.
  • The feature works offline with local encounter photos.
  • The UI supports one-photo encounters first; multi-photo behavior should be explicit if present, not accidental.
  • Instrument review_image_zoom_open, review_image_zoom_close, and optionally max zoom/elapsed time if lightweight.

Product Notes

  • This should be built as image inspection, not as a decorative full-screen gallery. The job is to let the user answer, "what am I actually seeing here?"
  • The first pass can be review-only if that is fastest, but the component should be reusable by encounter detail soon after.
  • Do not let zoom hide the review task permanently. The user needs a clean path back to the prompt/card.

Evidence Ledger

P1 - Zoom and pan the map view

Status: Backlog - candidate for next TestFlight follow-up Source: Andy in-field map-view usage, 2026-07-20 Area: Map view / spatial review door Type: Usability gap in the core place-memory loop

Problem

Andy wants to zoom into the map view, but the current map surface does not support zoom. That limits the map's usefulness as a spatial review door: pins can orient the user at a glance, but the user also needs to inspect local clusters, distinguish nearby encounters, and understand where a specific observation happened.

The map is not only decoration or trip atmosphere. It is a memory surface. If it cannot be zoomed and panned, it cannot carry the job we are asking it to do.

User Story

As a field user, I want to zoom and pan the map view, so I can inspect encounter locations and nearby clusters without losing the spatial context of the trip.

Why It Matters

  • Supports the map as a real review door, not a static postcard.
  • Helps users distinguish encounters that are geographically close together.
  • Makes GPS pins more useful when multiple captures accumulate in one area.
  • Preserves the product thesis that place is part of the memory cue, not just metadata.

Acceptance Criteria

  • In the full map view, the user can pinch-zoom and pan the map.
  • Encounter pins remain correctly anchored to their projected locations while zooming and panning.
  • Tapping a pin still opens the intended encounter or map-review path after zoom/pan.
  • The user can reset or naturally return to the default trip-level view without getting lost.
  • The feature works offline with the current bundled/static map approach.
  • If implemented before MapLibre, keep the projection math and touch handling isolated so the later native-map upgrade can replace the map layer cleanly.
  • Instrument map_zoom_start, map_zoom_end, and optionally max zoom level / pin tap after zoom if lightweight.

Product Notes

  • This is distinct from zooming encounter photos. Photo zoom supports visual evidence inspection; map zoom supports place-memory inspection.
  • The compact home map can remain a glanceable card for now. The full map view is the priority surface for zoom/pan.
  • If clusters become hard to tap after zoom lands, that is a follow-up signal for pin clustering or a list/map hybrid, not a reason to overbuild this first pass.

P1 - Dynamic themes and user-defined keywords

Status: Backlog - candidate for next TestFlight follow-up / product-model pass Source: Andy in-field capture/review usage, 2026-07-20 Area: Encounter tagging / knowledge model Type: Product-model gap in capture and review

Problem

The pre-filled themes and keywords are not proving very useful in real use. Andy is noticing other things in the field, but the current app asks him to sort observations through a fixed prototype taxonomy. That creates friction and weakens the record: the app captures what the prototype expected, not necessarily what the user actually noticed.

This is more than a tagging UI issue. Tags are part of the app's early knowledge model. If they are wrong or too generic, review, filtering, export, and future tutor behavior all inherit that weakness.

User Story

As a field user, I want to add and reuse my own themes and keywords on encounters, so the app reflects what I am actually noticing instead of forcing every observation into pre-filled categories.

Why It Matters

  • Makes capture feel like a field notebook, not a form with someone else's taxonomy.
  • Creates better raw material for review, filtering, export, and future tutor signals.
  • Lets the app learn from repeated user language and recurring trip/place patterns.
  • Keeps the prototype scaffold from hardening into product truth without evidence.

Acceptance Criteria

  • The user can add a custom keyword/tag while capturing or enriching an encounter.
  • Previously used tags are easy to reuse, with recent/frequent tags surfaced ahead of static defaults.
  • Pre-filled themes remain optional suggestions, not a closed list.
  • Existing theme tags continue to display and export correctly after the change.
  • Tags/keywords work offline and persist locally.
  • Export includes custom tags/keywords in a stable, readable structure.
  • The system distinguishes user-authored tags from app-suggested tags if that distinction is cheap to preserve.
  • Instrument tag_add_custom, tag_select_existing, and tag_suggestion_used if lightweight.

Product Notes

  • First pass should favor manual custom tags plus recent/frequent reuse. Do not jump straight to AI-generated taxonomy unless we have a clear signal that manual capture is too slow.
  • The current fixed themes can become starter suggestions. They should not be treated as the canonical domain model.
  • Watch for tag sprawl. The right follow-up may be gentle normalization/merge suggestions after enough usage, not aggressive constraints during capture.
  • This connects to the future tutor, but should not wait for the tutor. Better tags improve the field notebook immediately.

P1 Bug - Blank screen after Wispr Flow keyboard handoff during new encounter notes

Status: Backlog - needs reproduction details / candidate for next TestFlight bug pass Source: Andy in-field capture usage, 2026-07-20 Area: New encounter capture / notes input / app lifecycle Type: Capture-flow reliability bug

Problem

Andy starts a new encounter and begins taking notes. When he switches keyboards to use Wispr Flow, iOS moves him into the Wispr Flow app. After swiping left to return to Frost Rocks, the app comes back to what appears to be a blank screen.

This is a reliability problem in the capture loop. The user is trying to add field notes at the exact moment the app should be most forgiving, and the keyboard handoff appears to put Frost Rocks into a bad return state.

Reproduction Notes

  • Start a new encounter.
  • Enter the note-taking state.
  • Switch keyboard/input method to Wispr Flow.
  • iOS opens or foregrounds the Wispr Flow app.
  • Swipe left / return to Frost Rocks.

  • Andy can provide screenshots and more details later.

User Story

As a field user using dictation or an alternate keyboard, I want Frost Rocks to preserve the new encounter screen when I leave and return, so I can continue capturing notes without losing context or hitting a blank state.

Why It Matters

  • Protects the core capture workflow from app-switching interruptions.
  • Supports real-world note entry, where users may rely on dictation, third-party keyboards, or voice workflows.
  • Reduces risk of lost observations during a time-sensitive field moment.
  • Signals whether the app lifecycle handling around camera/input/navigation state is brittle.

Acceptance Criteria

  • Returning from Wispr Flow or another keyboard/helper app restores the same new encounter note state instead of a blank screen.

P1 - Capture user questions for later AI exploration

Status: Backlog - candidate for next TestFlight follow-up / tutor-loop discovery Source: Andy in-field noticing usage, 2026-07-20; Andy shorthand, 2026-07-21 Area: Capture notes / review / AI exploration Type: Product-model gap in the curiosity loop

Problem

Andy is finding that the things he wants to note are not only topics, tags, or rock identifications. They are often live questions that arise while looking at a place or object: "Why is it like that?" "Why is that boulder out in the middle?" The current capture model can store a note, but it does not treat questions as first-class material that can be revisited, answered, explored, or used to shape a review session.

This is a strong signal that Frost Rocks may be less about getting users to label rocks correctly in the moment and more about preserving curiosity before it evaporates. Identification is still part of the loop, but user-authored questions may be a better doorway into learning.

User Story

As a field user, I want to capture questions that occur to me while looking at rocks, landforms, or places, so I can explore them with AI either in the moment or during a later review session.

Why It Matters

  • Preserves the user's own curiosity, not just app-imposed categories.
  • Creates a natural bridge from field capture to AI tutor behavior.
  • Makes review feel inquiry-driven instead of only recall-driven.
  • Helps the app learn what the user actually wants to understand about geology and place.
  • Reduces pressure to know the right term before capturing the observation.

Acceptance Criteria

  • The user can mark or enter a captured note as a question during encounter capture or enrichment.
  • Questions remain visible on the encounter detail screen and are not flattened into generic notes or tags.
  • The review flow can surface unanswered or recently captured questions as prompts.
  • The user can start an AI exploration from a saved question, either immediately or later.
  • Question text is preserved exactly as the user wrote it, alongside any AI response or learning note.
  • Questions work offline as saved local data; AI exploration can wait until connectivity exists.
  • Export includes questions in a stable, readable structure distinct from tags and freeform notes.
  • Instrument question_capture, question_review_open, and question_ai_explore_start if lightweight.

Product Notes

  • Do not force every note into a question. The product should support observations, labels, hypotheses, and questions as neighboring capture types.
  • First pass can be simple: detect or let the user mark question-like notes, then collect them into review and AI exploration surfaces.
  • This connects directly to the future tutor thesis. The strongest tutor prompt may be the user's own question, not a generated quiz.
  • Watch for the right timing: in-the-moment AI can be valuable, but it should not interrupt field attention unless the user asks for it.
  • Andy's later shorthand to revisit: noticing, remembering, connecting. Treat this as a discussion seed, not a committed framework yet.
