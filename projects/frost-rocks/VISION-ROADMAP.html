Feature 1 — The AI Tutor

What it is

The move from "field notebook with memory" to "field notebook that teaches you." Today the app scaffolds (prompts, review doors, "look again" hints). The tutor is the next rung: something that diagnoses your confusions and actively works them, over time, across encounters.

The design line I will hold

My SOUL rule and the boundary note agree: veto the generic chatbot. A "chat with an AI about rocks" box is the lazy version and it's a boundary violation. The tutor earns its place only if every AI move maps to a specific learner confusion, a feedback loop, or a scaffold. Design test for every tutor feature: does it train the eye, or just answer the question? If it just answers, it's a photo-ID tool and we already said we're not that.

Where the tutor actually lives (the sharp version)

The tutor's real job is discrimination, not recall. Rock ID is a perceptual problem — the hard part is telling near-neighbors apart in the wild (granite vs. gneiss, basalt vs. gabbro). So the tutor's native unit is the confusion pair, not the individual rock. Concretely, the tutor should:

states, near-neighbor guesses — and infer which pairs the user actually confuses. (DESIGN.md already flags "confident-wrong = high-priority re-teach.")

What it is

Today the app is implicitly single-trip ("Finland 2026", "Day 3"). Multi-trip is the shift to a lifelong record: Finland, then Utah, then the North Shore again and again. The unit of the app stops being the trip and becomes your accumulating geological experience.

Why it's more than a data change

This is where the emotional thesis compounds. One trip is a notebook. Many trips is a life of noticing — and that's a far stronger engine than any single vacation. The design work is making accumulation feel like richness, not clutter.

Concept — Home Place vs. Visited Places (the North Shore idea)

This is the idea I'm most excited about from Andy's note, and I think it's the spine of the next era.

The insight

Not all places are equal. Finland and Utah are visited — rare, intense, one-off. The North Shore is home turf — recurring, seasonal, deepening. Andy will return to the North Shore many times; he'll see the same shoreline basalt across years and conditions. Treating all places identically throws away the single richest learning structure the app has access to.

Why it matters for design (all three of: map, review, tutor)

visited places as satellites/expeditions around it. Emotionally: here is my place, and here are the places I've been. (That's almost verbatim Andy's phrasing, and it's a strong map organizing idea.)

— the natural home of spaced, longitudinal, "you've seen this outcrop in four seasons" learning. A visited place is a burst: capture-heavy, reviewed intensely for a while, then consolidated into long-term memory. The scheduler should treat these two rhythms differently.

How these fit together (one paragraph)

Multi-trip provides the structure (many places over time). Home-vs-visited provides the meaning (which places are yours, and how learning rhythms differ). The tutor provides the engine (discrimination-first, confusion-pair learning that compounds because you now see the same rocks across many places and return to your home ground repeatedly). Live Map is the surface that makes all of it legible at a glance. None of these is really separable — which is why it's worth holding them in one roadmap rather than shipping them as four disconnected features.

Parked open question — enrich step: generative guess vs. passive confirm (added 2026-07-14)

From the weekly learning-science pass (capture-now / reflect-later). Andy asked to park this to come back to after Finland.

The question: should the Finland enrich step force a one-tap guess before reveal, or stay a passive confirm?

Why it matters (the trap): "a camera app that happens to be a field guide" is the right framing for engagement, but the shutter is the hook, not the lesson. The photo-taking-impairment effect (Henkel 2014) means the capture reflex can quietly offload attention — a beautiful shoebox of photos nobody learns from. The fix is NOT capture friction (that kills the reflex we built on purpose); it's relocating the desirable difficulty downstream into enrichment.

Two consequences to weigh when we pick this up:

(generation effect) beats a passive confirm, and doubles as the calibration signal. Same two-tap speed, radically different encoding.

with a real learning job (encoding specificity). That quietly raises D5's priority.

  • Enrichment should be generative — a one-tap "what do you think this is?" guess before reveal
  • The map / GPS pins (D5) aren't only emotional architecture — they're context-reinstatement cues

Decision hook: the enrich-vs-shot-and-go instrumentation already in v2 will otherwise only tell us whether we shipped a great camera with a hollow field guide inside it. Guess-before-reveal turns that same metric into a learning signal.

No spec change made — sharpens already-locked D1 (enrich-after-shutter) and D5 (map). Durable principle logged in Izzy's SOUL.md ("capture vs. reflection split"). 🧩
