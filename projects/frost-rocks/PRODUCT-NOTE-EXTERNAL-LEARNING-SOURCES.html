Product Note — External Learning Sources

Petra | 2026-07-07

Prompt

Andy raised a Frost Rocks idea ahead of the Finland trip: the app could let a user explicitly add learning they have been exposed to outside the app, such as a podcast, book, website, article, video, or conversation.

The immediate example was a podcast about high-accuracy GPS, isostatic rebound, and the Finland archipelago. Andy's observation: this kind of source is relevant to his geology/place knowledge even though it is not a rock encounter in the field.

Core Idea

Frost Rocks should consider letting users add external learning sources to their domain knowledge model.

The app would capture enough metadata and user context to understand that the user has been exposed to certain topics, then use that information to support later learning.

Possible uses:

  • Connect new observations to prior learning.
  • Seed or enrich spaced repetition review.
  • Build a more accurate model of what the user knows, has encountered, and is curious about.
  • Surface relevant past sources when the user logs a related field encounter.
  • Help the user see their geology understanding as a connected web, not a pile of isolated notes.

Product Strategy Read

This is strategically interesting because it expands Frost Rocks from "I found a rock" into "I am building geological understanding over time." That is much closer to the product thesis: skill development and place-based learning, not commodity object identification.

The important distinction: the source itself is not the product. The product value comes from turning exposure into usable memory, connection, and practice.

Initial Shape

A lightweight version could support a user saying:

  • I listened to this podcast.
  • I read this book or article.
  • I watched this video.
  • I visited this website.
  • I had this conversation.

For each source, Frost Rocks might capture:

  • Title or description.
  • Source type.
  • Date consumed.
  • Optional link, photo, transcript, notes, or citation.
  • User's short takeaway.
  • Topics/themes inferred by the app.
  • Confidence that the user understood or retained the material.
  • Related places, rocks, minerals, formations, processes, or prior encounters.

Example

Source: podcast about high-accuracy GPS and isostatic rebound in the Finland archipelago.

Potential inferred topics:

  • Isostatic rebound.
  • Post-glacial geology.
  • Baltic Sea / Finnish archipelago.
  • GPS / geodesy.
  • Land uplift.
  • Relationship between geology and place change over time.

Later, when Andy logs an observation in coastal Finland, the app could connect the encounter back to the earlier source:

> You listened to a podcast about isostatic rebound before the trip. This coastline is part of that same story: the land here is still rising after the last ice sheet retreated.

That is a strong learning moment if it appears at the right time and does not feel like trivia spam.

Product Questions

  • Does explicit source capture increase later understanding, or does it become another archive users neglect?
  • How lightweight must capture be for users to actually do it?
  • Should the app ask for the user's takeaway before inferring topics?
  • Should sources create review cards directly, or only enrich context until the user chooses what to practice?
  • What is the right unit of knowledge: source, concept, claim, place, encounter, or question?
  • How do we avoid pretending that exposure equals understanding?
  • Which source types matter most for the Andy archetype: podcasts, books, websites, videos, lectures, or conversations?

Risks

  • Users may not want to manually log media consumption.
  • Topic extraction can create false confidence if the app treats exposure as mastery.
  • Copyright and transcript handling can become complicated if we ingest full content.
  • This could widen the scope before the Finland prototype proves the core capture-review loop.
  • The feature could become a generic read-it-later or note-taking tool, which is not the strategic wedge.

Lean Validation Path

For the Finland prototype, do not build full ingestion.

Instead, test a very small source marker:

  • Add a manual "I learned about this" entry type, or
  • Let Andy attach a short note/source to the trip knowledge base, or
  • Pre-seed the Finland prototype with a few source-backed concepts from Andy's actual preparation.

Success signal: Andy later finds the connection useful during field capture or review, and it changes what he notices, remembers, or wants to investigate next.

Failure signal: the source sits in the app as inert reference material and does not affect observation, review, or understanding.

Strategic Lean

Worth preserving as a product direction, but not yet a core build requirement.

My recommendation: treat this as a knowledge-model enrichment hypothesis for the Finland prototype. Capture the source idea manually or semi-manually, observe whether it creates meaningful later connections, and only then decide whether external source ingestion deserves product surface area. ---

Running Log — 2026-07-07: Learning Connection Moment

Andy added a second example from the same Finland preparation podcast.

The podcast guest discussed isostatic rebound and used the idea of moment of inertia: as land rebounds after glacial unloading, mass distribution changes slightly outward from Earth's center. Andy understood this by connecting it to the classic ice skater analogy: arms out means higher moment of inertia and slower spin; arms in means lower moment of inertia and faster spin.

The important product signal is not only that Andy consumed content. It is that he noticed a moment of understanding forming:

  • New geology/geophysics topic: isostatic rebound affecting Earth's mass distribution.
  • Physics concept: moment of inertia.
  • Familiar analogy: spinning ice skater with arms extended or pulled in.
  • Learning event: "this clicked for me because I connected the new concept to an example I already knew."

This suggests a second class of user-captured knowledge artifact: a learning connection, not just an external source.

Possible shape:

  • "I learned something."
  • "This reminded me of..."
  • "This explains..."
  • "This connects to..."
  • "The example that made it click was..."

Strategic implication: Frost Rocks may eventually need to model not only what topics the user has encountered, but what bridges helped them understand those topics. Those bridges could become powerful review prompts because they preserve the user's own path to understanding.

Example future review prompt:

> You connected isostatic rebound to the ice skater moment-of-inertia example. How does moving mass outward affect spin?

Caution: keep this as running product discovery evidence for now. Do not promote it into Finland build requirements unless we decide to test a very small manual "learning connection" capture flow.
