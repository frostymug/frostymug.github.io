Layer A Concept Spine — Finland Place-Pack, v1

Status: DRAFT for Petra review → then ships as bundled data (concepts table, spine_version: finland-v1) Author: Izzy (Design) · 2026-07-22 Role: This is the authored curriculum behind the D3 tutor (D3-WEDGE-SPEC §8). The AI does not invent the curriculum at runtime; it teaches from this. Human-readable Markdown is the canonical source; it converts mechanically to the bundled JSON. Scope: the ~30 concepts a curious rock-hound actually meets in southern/central Finland, seeded from Andy's real 14 Finland encounters + WoZ pilot #1. Not a geology textbook — a field curriculum: every concept earns its place by being visible from a trail.

Design principles (carried from canon):

  • The confusion pair is the unit of instruction. Each concept lists what it gets mixed up with and the single resolving feature that settles it.
  • Diagnostic features are field-executable — things you can check with eyes, a fingernail, or tilting the rock in sunlight. No lab tests.
  • Sequencing = confusion distance: far-apart discriminations first (rock vs. mineral, granite vs. gneiss), near-neighbor pairs (gneiss vs. migmatite) as the eye sharpens. depends_on encodes the progression.
  • Every concept carries a field_check — the seed for the tutor's one look-again (D3-S3.4).

Format per concept: slug · family · one-liner — Diagnostics: field-visible features — Confused with: pair → resolving feature — Depends on · Field check

---

A. Reading rocks at all (framework)

mineral_vs_rock · framework

A mineral is one crystalline substance; a rock is a fabric of minerals. Granite isn't a thing — it's a crowd. — Diagnostics: if you can point at differently colored/lustered grains, you're looking at a rock made of several minerals. — Confused with: everyday usage where "mineral" = "rock" → resolving: one substance vs. an interlocking mixture. — Depends on: — · Field check: pick any coarse boulder and count the distinct grain types (usually 3: glassy, blocky-pale, dark).

interlocking_crystals · framework

In an igneous rock nothing is "inside" anything: all the crystals grew together as the melt froze, locking like puzzle pieces. — Diagnostics: grains share tight irregular boundaries; no rounded transported grains, no cement, no gaps. — Confused with: "inclusions" (the recurring learner word) → resolving: an inclusion is a foreign object trapped in a host; granite's crystals are all full citizens that grew simultaneously (see xenolith for the real thing). — Depends on: mineral_vs_rock · Field check: trace one crystal's outline — it fits its neighbors exactly, like dry-stone walling.

deep_time_shield · framework

Southern Finland's bedrock is ~1.9 billion years old — the Fennoscandian Shield, roots of a vanished mountain range, planed flat by erosion and ice. — Diagnostics: (context concept) hard crystalline rock everywhere, no young sediments on top, glacially smoothed surfaces. — Confused with: assuming visible landscape age = rock age → resolving: the surface shape is ~11,000 years old (ice); the rock itself is ~170,000× older. — Depends on: — · Field check: any outcrop: the material predates animals, the polish postdates mammoths.

svecofennian_orogeny · framework/process

"The great squeeze" (~1.92–1.79 Ga): the mountain-building that cooked and folded most of southern Finland's gneisses and migmatites. — Diagnostics: its signature is fabric — foliation, banding, folds — in the older rocks. — Confused with: rapakivi story → resolving: rapakivi intruded ~1.65–1.5 Ga, AFTER the squeeze, so it has no squeeze-fabric (the pilot's "pink is the pigment, texture is the plot"). — Depends on: deep_time_shield · Field check: deformed = survived the orogeny; relaxed texture = came later.

B. The minerals you actually meet (4 + 1 discrimination)

quartz · mineral

The glassy one. Hard, greasy-lustered, breaks like glass, no flat faces inside the rock. — Diagnostics: glassy/greasy luster; gray-translucent; no cleavage — never flashes flat faces; scratches steel (hardness 7); conchoidal (shell-like) fracture on broken surfaces. — Confused with: feldspar → resolving: tilt in light — quartz never flashes (see quartz_vs_feldspar). — Depends on: mineral_vs_rock · Field check: find the grains that stay greasy at every tilt angle.

feldspar · mineral

The blocky one — Earth's most abundant mineral family. Opaque, porcelain-like, pink (K-feldspar) or white-gray (plagioclase), breaks along flat internal planes. — Diagnostics: blocky/rectangular habit; porcelain luster; two cleavage directions → flat faces that flash like tiny mirrors when tilted in sunlight; pink or chalk-white. — Confused with: quartz → resolving: the flash (see quartz_vs_feldspar). — Depends on: mineral_vs_rock, mineral_cleavage · Field check: tilt slowly; catch a flash; that grain is feldspar.

k_feldspar_pink · mineral

The pink of southern Finland. Potassium feldspar is the pigment behind nearly every pink rock on a Finnish trail — pink is common, not diagnostic of any one rock. — Diagnostics: salmon-to-brick pink blocky grains; same cleavage flash as all feldspar. — Confused with: "pink rock = rapakivi" → resolving: pink tells you K-feldspar is present; the TEXTURE (ovoids? stretched lenses? blocky grains?) tells you which rock. — Depends on: feldspar · Field check: next three pink rocks: same pink, three different textures?

dark_minerals · mineral

The pepper in the salt-and-pepper: biotite mica (glittery black flakes, peels in sheets) and hornblende (duller black prisms). V1 treats them as one "dark minerals" concept; splitting comes later. — Diagnostics: biotite glitters and flakes; hornblende is matte and stubby; both scatter as specks/clots in granitoids, concentrate in dark bands in gneiss. — Confused with: each other (fine at this stage) → resolving (when ready): glitter-and-flake vs. matte-and-block. — Depends on: mineral_vs_rock · Field check: does the black grain glitter as you rotate it (biotite) or stay matte (hornblende)?

quartz_vs_feldspar · discrimination ★ (pilot-validated)

THE first-year discrimination, learnable in 30 seconds of sunlight. — Resolving feature: cleavage flash. Feldspar's flat cleavage faces flash like mirrors at the right tilt; quartz has no cleavage and stays glassy-greasy at every angle. (Learner's own phrasing, WoZ pilot #1: "flashy feldspar cleaves differently than greasy quartz.") — Depends on: quartz, feldspar, mineral_cleavage · Field check: the tilt test on any coarse granite.

C. Breakage: the cleavage/jointing cluster

mineral_cleavage · structure (crystal-scale)

How a crystal breaks: along flat planes set by its atomic structure. A property of minerals, visible as flat reflective faces at millimeter scale. — Diagnostics: flat, step-like or mirror-like faces within single grains; repeats at the same angles throughout a mineral species. — Confused with: jointing (the learner's own recorded confusion — two field notes) → resolving: scale and obedience — cleavage is mm-scale and follows the crystal; joints are meter-scale and ignore everything (see pair below). Also confused with weathering_pits → resolving: cleavage reflects light in flat sheets; pits scatter it. — Depends on: mineral_vs_rock · Field check: find a flashing feldspar face — that's cleavage; now find a crack crossing the whole boulder — that isn't.

jointing · structure (outcrop-scale) ★ (pilot-taught)

How a rock mass cracks: sets of roughly planar fractures from stress relief — the rock expanding as erosion and ice stripped kilometers of overburden ("unloading"). — Diagnostics: through-going cracks crossing the whole outcrop; occur in roughly parallel or perpendicular sets; cut across banding/foliation indifferent to it; no displacement (that would be a fault). — Confused with: mineral_cleavage → resolving: joints ignore the rock's fabric and cross all grains; cleavage obeys a single crystal's structure. Also "the rock was broken by something recent" → resolving: joint faces weather like the rest of the surface (lichen, rounding); fresh breaks are pale and sharp. — Depends on: mineral_cleavage (as contrast) · Field check: do the cracks respect the stripes or ignore them? Joints ignore.

unloading_exfoliation · process

Why jointing happens here: rock that crystallized under mountains is now at the surface; pressure release opens cracks, sometimes in onion-like sheets parallel to the ground. — Diagnostics: sheet-like slabs parallel to outcrop surfaces; perpendicular joint sets; freshest expression on glacially scoured whalebacks. — Confused with: frost wedging (which widens existing joints) → resolving: unloading creates the pattern; frost exploits it. — Depends on: jointing, deep_time_shield · Field check: slabs peeling parallel to the rock's skin = unloading; blocks pried along existing cracks = frost's follow-up work.

D. The granitoid family and its fabrics

granite · rock

Coarse interlocking quartz + feldspar + dark minerals, no oriented fabric. Texturally relaxed — it froze from melt and was never squeezed (or lost the fight evenly). — Diagnostics: salt-and-pepper (± pink); coarse visible grains; random orientation — no stripes, no aligned lenses. — Confused with: gneiss → resolving: fabric — granite has none, gneiss is banded/aligned. — Depends on: quartz, feldspar, interlocking_crystals · Field check: rotate your view 90° — does the rock look the same in every direction? Granite does.

granodiorite_gray_granitoid · rock

The gray cousins (granodiorite/tonalite): granite's recipe with more plagioclase and dark minerals, less pink K-feldspar. Most "gray granites" on Finnish trails. — Diagnostics: like granite but gray-white overall; little or no pink. — Confused with: granite (fine — same family; naming precision is v2) → resolving: proportion of pink; don't sweat it in the field. — Depends on: granite · Field check: how much pink? Lots = granite proper; almost none = gray granitoid.

foliation · structure ★ (pilot-reinforced)

Planar fabric from directed pressure: platy/elongate minerals rotated into alignment. The rock's grain, like wood. — Diagnostics: dark flakes and stretched lenses sharing one orientation; the rock splits more easily parallel to it; visible as streakiness even without full banding. — Confused with: surface scratches/striations → resolving: foliation goes THROUGH the rock (check a broken edge); scratches sit on the skin. — Depends on: dark_minerals, svecofennian_orogeny · Field check: find the direction the rock "wants" to split.

gneissic_banding · structure

High-grade metamorphism sorts minerals into alternating light (quartz-feldspar) and dark (biotite-hornblende) bands — foliation graduated into stripes. — Diagnostics: cm-scale light/dark stripes of mineral segregation; bands share the sparkle of visible crystals on both light and dark stripes. — Confused with: sedimentary bedding → resolving: bedding is grain-size layers in dull matrix (and doesn't sparkle); gneiss bands are crystalline and often folded. Also confused with vein_pegmatite → resolving: bands run parallel to the fabric; veins CUT it (cross-cutting = younger). — Depends on: foliation · Field check: does the pale stripe run with the fabric (band) or slice across it (vein)?

gneiss · rock

The banded veteran: granite-ish chemistry that survived the orogeny and wears the stripes to prove it. — Diagnostics: coarse crystalline + gneissic banding + foliation; often folded. — Confused with: granite → resolving: fabric; migmatite → resolving: are the bands orderly or swirled/discordant?; schist → resolving: gneiss = striped and granular; schist = all-over mica sheen that splits into slabs. — Depends on: granite, gneissic_banding · Field check: stripes + sparkle + fold = gneiss.

migmatite · rock ★ (pilot-taught; the Helsinki signature)

"Melted heart" rock: gneiss that got hot enough to partially melt. The pale melt fraction swirled like fudge ripple before refreezing — Helsinki's characteristic bedrock. — Diagnostics: banding gone unruly — wavy, pinching, discordant pale veins-and-swirls; ghost-stripes at slightly different angles to each other; often net-like pale patches. — Confused with: gneiss → resolving: orderly parallel stripes = gneiss; wandering, pinch-and-swell, mutually discordant stripes = migmatite. — Depends on: gneiss · Field check: follow one pale band with your finger — if it wanders, merges, or dead-ends, the rock was partly molten.

schist · rock

Mica-rich, medium-grade cousin (Tampere belt country): splits into flaky slabs, glitters all over. — Diagnostics: overall silvery/glittery sheen (mica everywhere); splits into plates; finer-grained than gneiss, no bold stripes. — Confused with: gneiss → resolving: sheen-and-split vs. stripe-and-granular. — Depends on: foliation · Field check: does the whole surface glitter and the rock break into plates? Schist.

rapakivi_granite · rock ★ (the trip quest)

Finland's famous granite (~1.65–1.5 Ga, post-orogenic): "crumbly rock," with the killer diagnostic being a shape. — Diagnostics: ovoids — rounded egg-shaped K-feldspar crystals, typically 1–3 cm (cherry to wine-cork; showy ones to golf-ball), classically rimmed with a thin pale-green plagioclase shell (wiborgite); texturally relaxed (no stretching); weathers grussy/crumbly. — Confused with: ordinary porphyritic granite → resolving: rounded ovoids ± rims vs. angular blocky phenocrysts; deformed pink granite/gneiss → resolving: rapakivi crystals sit round and undeformed — stretched lenses mean the rock predates the squeeze; xenolith → resolving: ovoid = one big crystal (uniform, flashes as one face); xenolith = a piece of DIFFERENT rock (grainy inside). — Depends on: k_feldspar_pink, svecofennian_orogeny · Field check: hunt polished building facades: pink eggs with pale rims, measles-visible from standing height. If you must lean in and squint, it probably isn't rapakivi.

vein_pegmatite · structure/rock

Late melt and fluids squirted into cracks: quartz veins (milky white, fine) and pegmatites (absurdly coarse granite — crystals from cm to fist-size). — Diagnostics: cuts across the host fabric (cross-cutting = younger); sharp margins; pegmatite = same granite minerals at cartoon scale. — Confused with: gneissic_banding → resolving: parallel-to-fabric = band, cross-cutting = vein. — Depends on: gneissic_banding · Field check: the pale stripe that ignores every other stripe is the vein.

xenolith · structure

The real "inclusion": a chunk of pre-existing rock swallowed by intruding magma and frozen in, like a raisin in dough. — Diagnostics: a patch with different grain size/color/fabric than its host, often dark, often rounded-edged; internally it's a rock, not one crystal. — Confused with: big phenocrysts/ovoids → resolving: crystal = one substance, one flash; xenolith = its own little rock with its own grains. — Depends on: interlocking_crystals, granite · Field check: look INSIDE the blob: grains (xenolith) or one clean face (crystal)?

folding · structure

Banding bent while the rock was hot and ductile — the orogeny's handwriting. — Diagnostics: stripes curving smoothly through hairpins and S-shapes; both band and fold share the crystalline texture (not draped sediment). — Confused with: flow shapes in water-worn rock → resolving: the STRIPES fold; erosion only sculpts the outer surface. — Depends on: gneissic_banding · Field check: find one band and follow it around the bend.

E. The ice layer (Finland's second story)

glacial_erratic · glacial

A boulder the ice carried and dropped — often kilometers from its source, often sitting improbably alone. — Diagnostics: loose (not attached to bedrock); can differ from local bedrock type; often perched on soil, moss, or bare pavement of a different rock. — Confused with: local outcrop → resolving: attached and continuous with the ground = bedrock; loose and alien = erratic. — Depends on: deep_time_shield · Field check: kick-test conceptually — does this rock emerge from the earth or rest on it?

glacial_polish_whaleback · glacial

Ice as belt sander: bedrock ground into smooth, rounded whalebacks (roches moutonnées) — gentle polished up-ice slope, quarried steep down-ice face. — Diagnostics: smooth rounded bedrock humps; asymmetry (smooth stoss / broken lee) records ice-flow direction. — Confused with: water-worn rock → resolving: rivers polish channels and potholes; ice polishes whole hilltops far from any stream. — Depends on: deep_time_shield · Field check: smooth side vs. shattered side — the ice flowed FROM the smooth side.

glacial_striations · glacial

Scratches from stones dragged in the ice's sole: straight, parallel gouges on polished bedrock, all telling the same compass story. — Diagnostics: straight, parallel, often multiple sets; on polished surfaces; weathered to the same patina as the surface (not fresh). — Confused with: recent tool/machinery scratches → resolving: patina and company — striations are old, weathered, and come in consistent parallel families across the whole outcrop. — Depends on: glacial_polish_whaleback · Field check: find two scratches; if every neighbor agrees on direction, thank the ice.

esker · glacial

A river that flowed inside the ice, its gravel bed left behind as a sinuous ridge when the ice vanished. Finland's roads and campgrounds love them. — Diagnostics: long, narrow, winding gravel ridge, often tree-lined, often carrying a road or path; loose rounded stones inside. — Confused with: ordinary hills/moraines → resolving: eskers snake and stay narrow, like a buried river turned inside out. — Depends on: glacial_erratic (ice context) · Field check: if the trail runs a ridgeline that curves like a river, you may be walking an old under-ice riverbed.

postglacial_rebound · glacial/process

The land is still rising — ~mm/year in the south, nearly a cm in the northwest — because the crust is springing back from the ice's weight. Finland grows by square kilometers a year. — Diagnostics: (landscape concept) raised beaches and shorelines above the modern sea; harbors that shallowed historically; coastal meadows emerging. — Confused with: sea-level change stories → resolving: here it's mostly the LAND moving, not the water. — Depends on: deep_time_shield · Field check: by the Baltic shore: terraces of old beach lines climbing inland = the land's slow exhale.

weathering_pits · process

Small hollows where grains rot and drop out — texture of decay, not structure. — Diagnostics: scattered irregular pits following weaker grains; scatter light dully; deepest on exposed faces. — Confused with: mineral_cleavage faces (flagged in brief §4.4 example) → resolving: cleavage reflects (flat, shiny); pits absorb (rough, shadowed). Also vesicles (gas bubbles — rare in Finland's plutonics) → resolving: vesicles are smooth-walled and rounded; pits are ragged. — Depends on: mineral_cleavage · Field check: does the hollow have a flat flashing floor (cleavage face) or a crumbly one (pit)?

  • Biotite vs. hornblende split — same logic; dark_minerals holds the slot.
  • Diabase/dolerite dikes, amphibolite — genuinely present in Finland but none in the current 14 encounters; add on first sighting (spine is versioned).
  • Marine/Baltic sediment story, Litorina shorelines — belongs to postglacial_rebound depth, T1 material.
