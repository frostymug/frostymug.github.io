Frost Rocks — Design Document

Project: Geology and place-based learning app Company: Aspen Frost Consulting Design lead: Izzy Last updated: 2026-06-28 Status: Unlocked working frame

---

Current Design Read

Frost Rocks began as a rock identification skill-development app: field encounters seed at-home practice, and the user gets better at identifying rocks over time.

That thesis still matters.

The Finland prototype adds a sharper near-term test: can a real trip seed durable geology/place learning without making the trip feel like homework?

For now, Frost Rocks should be treated as:

> A field notebook with memory, built around geology learning and place-based curiosity.

The app should help the user notice, capture, revisit, and consolidate what they are learning about rocks, landforms, and the geological story of a place. It should not become a generic travel journal, and it should not compete head-on with instant rock ID tools.

---

Audience

  • Curious adult rock/geology hobbyists — birder energy
  • Intrinsically motivated, self-directed, and not pursuing credentials
  • Often in a life-transition stage with time and appetite for deeper hobbies
  • Interested in nature, travel, place, memory, and real-world learning
  • Comfortable with phones, but does not want the phone to dominate time outdoors
  • Wants to get better, not just outsource every answer to an app

Andy remains user zero for the Finland prototype.

---

Product Spine

Durable Frost Rocks Thesis

The durable product thesis is still off-field skill development:

  • In the field: photograph a rock/geology encounter -> attempt an observation or ID -> log to place
  • At home: encounter becomes practice, reflection, comparison, or review
  • Over time: field experience shapes the learning deck and learning changes future noticing
  • Motivation: geographic and experiential context make practice feel like memory, not drills

Finland Prototype Iteration

For the Finland user-zero prototype, soften the loop:

  • Notice: the user sees a place, rock, landform, built stone, or geological clue that sparks curiosity
  • Capture: photo, location, timestamp, event, and a quick note
  • Commit: the user records what they notice, guess, or wonder before receiving help
  • Scaffold: the app offers a small prompt that directs attention
  • Connect: the encounter is linked to a Finland geology/place theme
  • Reconstruct: later review starts with memory and original thinking
  • Refine: the user updates what they learned and what remains uncertain

The prototype should feel like a field notebook with memory, not a quiz app with photos.

---

Instructional Design Principles

  • Notice first, explain second. The first learning move is not "what is this?" but "what do you notice?"
  • Attempt first, always. The user should generate an observation, guess, or question before the app explains. This supports the generation effect and makes learning active.
  • Use recognition for app mechanics. Controls, states, available actions, and review modes should be visible. Do not make users remember how the interface works.
  • Use recall deliberately for learning. Memory, retrieval, comparison, and explanation are useful learning difficulty when they target the concept being learned.
  • Fading scaffolding. Novices get perceptual, vocabulary, and elimination help; more experienced users get less.
  • Perceptual scaffolding fades last. Noticing the right feature is the skill.
  • Uncertainty is respectable. "Likely," "possible," and "unknown" are valid states. A thoughtful unknown is better than a fake answer.
  • Error correction sequence. Validate reasoning -> isolate one distinguishing feature -> make the user look again -> resurface soon.
  • Confident-wrong matters. Confident wrong answers are high-priority re-teach events.
  • Confidence before reveal. Sure / Kind of / Guessing supports both scheduling and metacognitive calibration.
  • Field capture must be light. In-field interaction should increase connection to place, not steal attention from it.

---

Useful Learning Difficulty

Friction is not automatically bad design.

Remove friction that interferes with capture, navigation, saving, offline use, or emotional connection to the place.

Preserve or introduce friction when it creates:

  • Retrieval
  • Discrimination
  • Reflection
  • Calibration
  • Mental model building
  • Consolidation of previous knowledge

For the Finland prototype, the desirable difficulty should live mainly in:

  • Writing what was noticed
  • Naming uncertainty
  • Making a guess
  • Reconstructing the moment later
  • Comparing current understanding to the original observation

It should not live in:

  • Finding the save button
  • Completing too many fields outdoors
  • Waiting on connectivity
  • Being forced through a formal quiz while traveling

---

Finland Prototype Design Direction

Trigger To Open The App

Andy said the strongest triggers are:

  • A place he wants to remember
  • A question he cannot answer
  • A question he has about the place
  • Something beautiful or curiosity-provoking that makes him want to know more

This means the product should orient around curiosity and memory, not just identification.

Scope Boundary

Keep the prototype focused on geology.

Lakes, forests, buildings, roads, churches, monuments, and broader natural-history context may appear when they explain or enrich the geological story, but they should not become the primary object of the prototype.

Expansion vision to preserve:

> A generalized naturalist field journal for place-based learning.

That is adjacent future territory, not the Finland prototype center.

Offline / AI Fallback

Bad signal is the most important named trip constraint.

The app should support offline capture even if AI-generated context requires connectivity later.

Minimum fallback:

  • Save photo, timestamp, location, event, note, guess/question, and confidence locally
  • Mark the encounter as needing later context
  • Allow the user to return to the place instead of waiting on the app
  • Generate or fetch learning notes when connectivity returns

Open issue:

> What happens when the user wants AI help but has no connectivity?

End-Of-Day Reflection

Andy likes a short end-of-day reflection.

Purpose:

  • Consolidate the day's learning
  • Preserve questions
  • Surface patterns across encounters
  • Plant seeds for later review after the trip

Export / Journal Artifact

Andy wants an exportable trip artifact.

He already uses Diarium for journaling/diary. Frost Rocks should explore export or integration paths so encounters and reflections can flow into that existing journaling practice.

---

Primary Surfaces

1. Trip Notebook

Purpose: orient the user around the current trip.

Core elements:

  • Trip title, e.g. Finland 2026
  • Timeline of encounters
  • Map/list toggle
  • Today's captures
  • Event labels
  • Prompt of the day
  • Quiet review count
  • Theme filters

Design stance: calm field notebook, not dashboard.

2. Quick Capture

Purpose: capture quickly without killing the moment.

Required:

  • Photo
  • Auto location
  • Auto timestamp
  • "What did you notice?"

Optional:

  • Initial guess or question
  • Confidence: Sure / Kind of / Guessing
  • Event tag
  • Ask me later toggle
  • Offline save indicator

Target: useful capture in under 30 seconds.

3. Encounter Detail

Purpose: connect a logged moment to learning.

Core elements:

  • Photo gallery
  • Place/time/event
  • Original observation or guess
  • Original confidence
  • Uncertainty status: unknown / possible / likely / identified
  • Short learning note
  • Observation prompts
  • "What I learned later"
  • Theme tags

Design stance: this is the memory object.

4. Review At Home

Purpose: turn trip memories into durable knowledge.

Review should combine:

  • A little quiz, for retrieval
  • A little interview, for reflection
  • A little story reconstruction, for place memory

Reveal sequence:

  • Photo and place prompt
  • Memory question
  • Original note
  • Feature or concept prompt
  • Learning note
  • Reflection: what changed?
  • Scheduling: Review soon / Review later / I know this now

Design stance: returning to the trip, not being assigned homework.

---

Spaced Repetition: FSRS (Future-Relevant)

The Finland prototype does not need a full spaced repetition implementation if that slows learning, but FSRS remains relevant for the durable Frost Rocks skill loop.

Reference:

  • Repo: https://github.com/open-spaced-repetition/py-fsrs
  • Algorithm: FSRS — models stability, how long the user will remember, and difficulty, how hard an item is for the user, separately

Possible mapping:

  • Guessing + Wrong -> Again
  • Sure + Wrong -> Again, flagged as high-priority re-teach
  • Guessing + Right -> Hard
  • Kind of + Right -> Good
  • Sure + Right -> Easy

Important design constraint:

The user should not see the FSRS machinery. Confidence, recall, correctness, and review behavior can feed scheduling behind the scenes.

Possible mastery proxy:

  • FSRS stability value: days until forgetting
  • Continuous signal, not a graduation ceremony

For Finland, a simpler scheduling model may be enough:

  • Review soon
  • Review later
  • I know this now

---

Domain Knowledge Model

The knowledge model still needs four layers:

  • Taxonomy — igneous/sedimentary/metamorphic -> subtypes -> individual rocks, structured for learnability rather than pure geological completeness
  • Identifying features per rock — diagnostic features, not just full attribute lists
  • Confusion graph — which rocks get confused with which, and which features resolve each confusion
  • Feature salience by photo — what can be seen in a photo versus what requires physical examination

For Finland, add a prototype-level content layer:

  • Geological themes: glaciation, ancient bedrock, granite/gneiss, erratics, lakes/shorelines, exposed rock, built stone
  • Encounter types: bedrock, loose stone, shoreline, landform, building stone, memorial/monument, road/path
  • Review prompts: memory, feature noticing, uncertainty, mental model building

The domain model is the curriculum. The confusion graph remains especially important for long-term rock ID skill.

User Knowledge Mapping

The durable product needs a back-end model of what the user appears to know, not just a list of saved encounters.

Each encounter should be mapped against a known geology domain model:

  • The user's observation, question, guess, and confidence
  • The geological concepts implicated by that encounter
  • The diagnostic features the user noticed or missed
  • The confusions the encounter exposes, such as granite vs. gneiss or surface pattern vs. mineral texture
  • The evidence quality available from the photo, place, and any later reflection
  • The current learning state for the user: new, shaky, improving, reliable, or stale

This model should mostly remain invisible to the user. The interface may show gentle traces, such as "this connects to something you learned earlier," but the main purpose is back-end adaptation:

  • Choosing useful review prompts
  • Deciding whether the guide or the user should pick the next review starting point
  • Connecting trip memories into larger geology concepts
  • Feeding spaced repetition later, where FSRS schedules mapped concepts, confusions, or diagnostic features rather than isolated flashcards

Open design question:

> What is the smallest useful domain model for the Finland prototype: concept tags, confusion pairs, diagnostic features, or a richer graph?

---

Open Questions

- Is the decisive signal capture frequency, review return rate, quality of observations, emotional connection to Finland, or improved geology discrimination?

  • Finland success metric

- How far can the app lean into place/natural-history context before it stops being Frost Rocks?

  • Landscape learning boundary

- Should AI draft learning notes after the user's observation? - How do we prevent AI from short-circuiting the attempt-first learning loop? - What is the offline fallback when AI is unavailable?

  • AI assist

- Should the Finland starter pack be organized by geology concept, encounter type, place/event, or a hybrid?

  • Content model

- What is the right blend of quiz, interview, and story reconstruction?

  • Review mode

- What export format is most useful? - Is Diarium integration possible or desirable for early prototype scope?

  • Journal export

- What does "knowing a rock" mean in this product? - When should FSRS or another scheduler become necessary? - How much mastery/progress should be visible to the user?

  • Mastery model

- Where do reference photos come from? - Should user-submitted field photos feed back into future practice?

  • Photo sourcing

- Is progression purely self-directed, or should the app show structured growth? - How do we show improvement without making the product feel like school?

  • Assessment

---

Current Recommendation

For Finland, build around encounter memory, not identification.

Keep rocks and geology as the spine. Let place, curiosity, and travel memory supply the motivation. Preserve the deeper skill-development machinery, including FSRS and the confusion graph, as future layers once the field-notebook/review loop proves it has oxygen.

Design Log — Finland v2 Composite (2026-07-09, Izzy)

Andy selected across the A/B/C/D explore round; this composite assembles his mix.

  • Aesthetic: A (Rugged Field Guide)
  • Structure: B (timeline home spine); map shrunk to a compact card (still emotional architecture)
  • Knowledge rep: honest histogram/progress bars (over D's knowledge graph)
  • Capture: camera-first, two-tap (tap Capture → live camera → shutter); library + back as escape hatches out of the fast path; enrichment (notice/guess/confidence) moved after the shutter
  • Review: three doors shipped together and instrumented separately — map pin (spatial), entry button (temporal), nav tab (deliberate practice)
  • Instrumentation: prototype-only event log + live telemetry panel (tap→shutter ms, library-bail rate, enrich-vs-shot-and-go %, review-door counts, revisit follow-through). localStorage only, exportable JSON, no backend.

Reframe named for Andy: Frost Rocks is a camera app that happens to be a field guide.
