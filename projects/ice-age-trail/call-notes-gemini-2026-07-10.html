IATA App Check-in - Gemini Notes

Date of call: 2026-07-10 10:33 CDT Participants invited: Lisa Siewert and Andy Frost Source: Google Doc IATA App: Check-in - 2026/07/10 10:33 CDT - Notes by Gemini Source ID: 1NT_QjCmlQzFw03SNatHr8ZtmgYufjPqDA0FUaMWDKmw Preserved by: Aspen on 2026-07-11

Status

This is Gemini's meeting-notes artifact, not a verbatim transcript. Use it as a call record for planning and follow-up. Any exact quotes or legally sensitive conclusions should be checked against the recording or a future transcript.

Summary

Technical architecture review prioritized data layer development and addressed critical migration strategies for institutional security.

Participants analyzed current backend dependencies on university accounts and prioritized establishing independent data layers over application features. They decided to migrate data to an external account to secure project assets.

The team reviewed hosting options for backend services and explored methods to automate data siphoning to independent locations. Efforts focus on ensuring data portability while maintaining compliance with current institutional funding requirements.

Discussions covered logistical hurdles of board membership applications and the ongoing hiring process for a student project. The project roadmap remains centered on expanding geological data layers during the upcoming phase.

Decisions Captured By Gemini

  • Prioritize data layer development before continuing further application development.
  • Migrate project data infrastructure from UW-Stevens Point toward Lisa's Minnesota State account as the safer near-term destination.

Next Steps Captured By Gemini

  • Andy: draft hosting options for project layers and potential migration paths from current University of Wisconsin infrastructure.
  • Andy: send options analysis for Lisa's review.
  • Lisa: hire a student to work on the bedrock geology layer.
  • Lisa: post the job listing for bedrock geology digitization work.
  • Lisa: migrate work tasks from University of Wisconsin to Minnesota State where appropriate.
  • Lisa: document the procedure used to switch work between institutions and provide notes to Andy.

Detailed Notes

  • The current app relies on backend services bundled under an ArcGIS login. Parking information appears to come from Ice Age Trail Alliance sources, while geology layers use Esri/ArcGIS services. The front-end can be hosted independently, but the application still depends on those backend services.
  • Lisa secured a $5,000 grant to hire a student for a senior project focused on the bedrock geology layer. This would expand data beyond current compilations. Lisa clarified that bedrock geology is separate from glacial geology and geological points of interest, but all three layers represent original work that can be expanded.
  • Lisa has been encouraged to apply for an organization board seat because of her geological expertise. The board indicated that paid work on the application while serving as a board member would create a conflict of interest, so Andy and Lisa agreed paid work in that capacity is not feasible.
  • The next project phase separates front-end migration from backend/data-layer migration. Andy confirmed the front-end was successfully deployed to a free-tier Cloudflare account, while backend services remain under consideration as a phase-two architecture question.
  • Lisa emphasized that she is less attached to the application itself than to the development of the data layers. Both participants agreed that establishing durable data layers first is the logical path; the app can be built or rebuilt around the data later.
  • Andy and Lisa reviewed the codebase, including app.js and REST files that link to ArcGIS feature layers. Lisa explained that the feature layers function as the database for displaying topology. The current feature layers are owned by the University of Wisconsin-Stevens Point organization account, though Lisa created the data.
  • Lisa expressed concern about ownership of work created on university time and payroll. She prefers moving the data to a safer or more independent location. Potential destinations discussed included Ice Age Trail Alliance infrastructure, a private account, or Lisa's Minnesota State account.
  • Lisa suggested using her Minnesota State account as a practical third option, where copyright and data ownership concerns appear less restrictive. Lisa committed to moving data from UW-Stevens Point to Minnesota State and documenting the steps for Andy.
  • Lisa and Andy discussed hiring a student to help keep development moving. Andy planned to provide hosting options so Lisa can prepare for student interviews and explain the database/layer structure the student will use.
  • Lisa confirmed that the hired student will need to use the university ArcGIS account for data entry to satisfy supervision and funding requirements. Andy and Lisa discussed periodically copying or siphoning the data to an independent location as protection.
  • Andy and Lisa discussed segments.json, a distinct data layer that links trail segments to supplemental information for users. Lisa described it as originally created for teachers and students. The current JSON format is quick and dirty, but the intent is to expand this feature alongside the other data layers.
  • Lisa clarified the difference between ArcGIS feature layers, which are georeferenced, and segment data, which is not georeferenced but linked by name. Segment data currently covers a limited number of sections, with a goal of eventual expansion across all trail segments.
  • Andy brainstormed automated migration methods, including a tool to siphon data from the university ArcGIS account into a different location. Lisa was enthusiastic and confirmed that as owner of the layers she has the ability to manage and move the data.
  • Lisa described the board application process as extensive: resume, letter of interest, interview, vote by existing board members, and potentially a chapter-wide vote.
  • Andy estimated that the data layers are likely small enough for a free-tier ArcGIS account. Lisa noted ArcGIS pricing is hard to reason about and that she currently uses professional accounts through institutions, but she is willing to explore free-tier viability.
  • Lisa shared concerns about instability at the University of Wisconsin, including unpaid wages and lack of communication. This reinforced the decision to move project data to the Minnesota State account.
  • Lisa and Andy also discussed Finland geology, Andy's daughter's academic work, and Andy's upcoming trip. Those are useful relationship/context notes but not central to the Ice Age Trail architecture decision.
