This brief defines what we say, to whom, against what, to drive what action — before any design or copy. It is the strategic spine the website hangs on. Aesthetics and layout come after this is approved.

1. Who We Are

Aspen Frost — agentic software. One person and a team of AI agents building real software, in public, like a real company.

Not an AI consultancy. Not a "we leverage AI to accelerate your transformation" shop. We are an experiment that's working: a single builder (Andy) who designed a structured system for getting AI agents to produce actual, shippable software — and is proving it by shipping.

The company name is Aspen Frost Agentic Software. The wordmark is Aspen Frost; "agentic software" is the descriptor beneath it. We never use the word "empire" externally — that's an internal, tongue-in-cheek thing. Externally we are simply a software company that happens to be run a very new way.

2. Who It's For (right now)

The honest answer: the website's first audience is us, and the people Andy chooses to show it to. Aspen Frost is still closer to passion project than functioning business — but it is deliberately traveling that ramp. The site should make the trajectory legible without pretending we're already at the destination: serious enough to feel real, honest enough to admit the business is still becoming.

Primary audience (now):

  • Andy — pride and proof. "Holy shit, we actually did this."
  • The team — the site makes the operation feel real and worth doing well.
  • The trusted few Andy shows it to — archetype: Erik & Amy. Erik (a clinician with a mental-

health project idea) and Amy (a small-business owner). The reaction we're designing for: they start by giggling and end by thinking "wait — Andy could actually build this for us."

Secondary audience (later, not yet):

  • Friends-and-family project stakeholders who might commission real work.
  • Eventually: people who'd pay for the apps, or a small custom build. This is a future phase.

We do not design the v1 site to chase external clients. We design it to be ready to point outward when the business is real.

NOT the audience (yet): enterprise buyers, eng leaders shopping dev shops, VC. We are not pitching them. If they wander in, the work should impress them — but they are not who we write for.

[needs-Petra / later] Real customer segmentation, buyer psychology, competitive alternatives, and willingness-to-pay are a later strategy phase. This brief intentionally does NOT assert them.

3. The Wedge

The one thing we can credibly claim that others in the conversation can't.

It is not "we use AI agents." That is becoming table stakes. Anyone can spin up a few agents and get a pile of confident slop.

The wedge is the system. Andy designed a structured operating system for agentic software — defined roles, adversarial review, a real build pipeline (design → architecture → build → deploy), handoffs, and a trust ramp. That system is what turns "one guy and some agents" into actual shippable software. The proof is a real product in real use.

Wedge, one line:

One person and a team of agents, building real software you can actually use — held together by a system, not held together by hope.

We never name the system publicly ("Empire OS" stays internal). We show the evidence of it and let the visitor conclude it's real.

4. The Three-Layer Slow Reveal

The site is built as a slow reveal. A first-time visitor experiences the work before they understand how it's made. The reveal has three layers, in this order:

  1. "Huh, nice software." The work looks legitimate. Real apps, real use. (Projects)
  2. "Wait — it's one guy and a team of agents?" The double-take. (About)
  3. "...oh. It's not chaos. There's a system behind this." The mic drop. Roles, review, pipeline.

This is the layer that converts skepticism into interest. (How We Work)

Layer 3 is where the credibility lives. Layers 1–2 earn the right to deliver it.

The agents speaking in their own distinct voices is the proof layer 3 is real. If they all sounded the same, it's one person with a thesaurus. Different voices, distinct roles, visibly able to disagree — that's a functioning team. The voices are the evidence.

5. Voice & Register

Serious about the craft. Humble about the certainty. Quietly delightful about the setup.

  • Confident about the work, never boastful. We show receipts instead of adjectives.
  • Humble and curious — "humilosity." This is an experiment in public. "We've gotten good

results, and we're still learning." The emotional core isn't humility alone — it's humility fused with curiosity. We're not modest because we're meek; we're modest because we genuinely believe the market is smarter than our taste, and we're hungry to find out why. That fusion has a name internally — humilosity — and it's strong enough to become a brand object on the site (see §7, Culture).

  • The delight is in the situation, never in the software. We never make jokes about the product.

We let the premise (one person, a team of agents, a marketing fish) be charming on its own and play it straight. Restraint is the sophistication. Anyone can be cute about AI; almost nobody has the confidence to just show the real thing and let it land.

Anti-brand (what would make Andy close the tab):

  • Buzzword soup. Too many mentions of "AI." "Leverage," "synergy," "transformation," "revolutionize."
  • Trying too hard to be cute or clever.
  • Corporate fog — copy that sounds like it could belong to any company.
  • Overselling — claiming to be a company we're not yet.

The read-aloud test applies to every line on this site. If you can't say it the way a human says it, it's not done.

Top to bottom, what a visitor should absorb in order:

  1. Hero / one true line. The idea to lodge: *"Huh — maybe one guy and a team of agents really can

build high-quality software."* (Exact wording TBD; this is the idea we protect.)

  1. The work is real. Frost Fitness is live and in real use. Honest status labels on everything else.
  2. The team is real — and surprising. One person, a team of agents, each with a role and a voice.
  3. The system is real — and still learning. How we work: a genuine pipeline with checks and balances,

The Humilosity device (Culture centerpiece)

Andy's note, and it's a good one: humilosity isn't a footnote, it's the heart. Treat it as a brand object — the word set as a dictionary entry, in a serif/dictionary font, with syllabic pronunciation. A reader who slows down for three seconds walks away with the whole philosophy in one glance.

hu·mil·os·i·ty  /(h)yo͞o-mi-ˈlä-sə-tē/  n. humility + curiosity, fused. The conviction that the market is smarter than your taste — and the appetite to go find out why. The operating belief of Aspen Frost.

It earns its keep three ways: it's ownable (you won't find it elsewhere), it's on-thesis (a made- up-but-true word for a made-up-but-real company), and it does the voice work in one object — serious about craft, humble about certainty. Hand this to Izzy as a hero moment on Culture; let it echo on How We Work as the why behind adversarial review. (Final wording/pronunciation is a copy+design task.)

8. Call to Action

No hard sell. The impression is the conversion (for now).

  • Primary: "Follow." The journey is the story; watching an experiment become real is the hook.

Caveat: placeholder only. We wire the button for later. We do NOT fake a feed/blog we won't maintain — a stale "latest post 4 months ago" actively damages the "this is real" thesis. The blog is an idea (Andy + Zev, someday), not a v1 commitment.

  • Secondary: "Contact." Quiet. A simple way for an Erik/Amy-type to start a conversation or submit

an idea. Not prominent, not salesy.

If we can only ship one well, ship the impression and a working Contact; defer Follow until the blog is real.

8b. The Next-Rung CTA — "Cover the Tokens" (defined, NOT live on v1)

Andy's framing (2026-06-03), and it's sharper than a side-note — it's the CTA the rest of the brief was building toward. This is documented here as the next rung on the trust/stakes ramp. It does not go live on the v1 site.

The offer, in his words: "There are people who always wanted to hire a developer or a software firm, but it was never affordable. Now it is — especially in the early days. I'll build your idea, give you my time for free, and just ask you to cover the cost of the tokens."

Why it's strategically strong (not just generous):

  • It's a reframe nobody's heard: "custom software — you just cover the compute." It rewrites the

cost structure of building software. That's a wedge in its own right.

  • It's honest — it maps exactly to where the operation is: still closer to passion project than

functioning business, but deliberately traveling the ramp toward real revenue.

  • It's the perfect early-ramp step: low cost to both sides, real portfolio, real learning, real

receipts — the thing that turns "huh, neat" into "wait, build mine."

  • It turns the site's quiet "Contact" into a genuine value proposition instead of a polite afterthought.

Why it is NOT on v1 — two gates, both above my pay grade:

  1. Stakes ramp. The moment this is public, you've invited real people with real expectations and

(often) real data. EMPIRE-OS requires an explicit conversation before taking medium-stakes work. Turning this CTA on is a stakes-ramp decision (Andy + Aspen), not a copy decision (Zev).

  1. Capacity honesty. "I'll give my time for free" is a promise. With a day job, the realistic

throughput is a couple of projects, not a faucet. An offer you can't fulfill damages the "this is real" thesis worse than no offer at all. When it goes live, it needs a built-in throttle — "taking on a couple of early projects" — not an open invitation.

The recommendation: design v1 so this CTA can be switched on the day Andy decides — the page structure, the Contact flow, and the messaging slot all exist and stay dormant. The site shows where we're going without overpromising where we are. When the stakes-ramp conversation happens, the copy is a short sprint, not a redesign.

[gate] Turning on the "cover the tokens" CTA = explicit Andy + Aspen stakes-ramp decision. Until then: documented direction, not live copy.

9. Aesthetic Direction (for Izzy — creative direction, not final design)

Anchors from Andy:

  • Background: ecru / warm paper, in the spirit of Anthropic/Claude's palette.
  • Accent: deep forest green where Claude uses orange. Green is the signature.
  • Motifs: trees, mountains, nature. Denver roots.
  • Soul: "Aspen" is a nature name and the name of Andy's late golden retriever. Warmth in the DNA.

Not literal, not on a billboard — but it's why the brand feels warm rather than cold-tech.

Direction: warm, calm, confident, natural. The opposite of neon-cyberpunk-AI. Trustworthy and human, with restraint. This is creative direction for Izzy to interpret, not a spec.

10. Open Questions (iterate; do NOT pre-solve)

  • Pronoun strategy — "I" vs "we." Load-bearing, not cosmetic. "I" tells the true/humble story

(one person); "we" tells the impressive/credible story (a real operation). Over-index "I" → undersell the differentiator. Over-index "we" → oversell / pretend to be a company we're not. Working hypothesis: "I" for Andy's voice and origin story, "we" for the team and the work — and let the wobble between them be visible (it pays off in the slow reveal). Decision: iterate on real copy, not in the abstract. Andy wants to be deliberate here.

  • Merge vs. separate "Culture" and "How We Work."
  • Exact hero line (idea is locked in §6; wording is a copy task).
  • When/whether the blog becomes real (gated on Andy's bandwidth + follow-through commitment).

11. What's NOT on the Site (v1 guardrails)

  • No "empire." Internal word only.
  • No Andy last name, no day job, no LinkedIn. He is just "Andy." Hiding in plain sight.
  • No naming Erik / Amy / Curate / Babycito without explicit green light. Anonymized: "a mental

health project," "a small business app."

  • No pricing. Nothing to buy yet; don't invent it.
  • No fake feed / dead blog. See §8.
  • No external-client positioning. We are ready to point outward later, not pitching now.
  • No overclaiming. Honest status labels on every project. Experiment in public, still learning.

Aspen Frost is one person and a team of AI agents building real software, in public, like a real company — before anyone said they were allowed to. The proof is the work. The surprise is the team. The reason it works is the system underneath — and we're still learning it out loud. Serious about the craft, humble about the certainty, quietly delightful about the setup. We never say "empire." We show the receipts and let you conclude: huh — maybe one guy and some agents really can.
