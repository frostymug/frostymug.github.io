This brief defines what we say, to whom, against what, to drive what action — before any design or copy. It is the strategic spine the website hangs on. Aesthetics and layout come after this is approved.

1. Who We Are

Aspen Frost — agentic software. One person and a team of AI agents building real software, in public, like a real company.

Not an AI consultancy. Not a "we leverage AI to accelerate your transformation" shop. We are an experiment that's working: a single builder (Andy) who designed a structured system for getting AI agents to produce actual, shippable software — and is proving it by shipping.

The company name is Aspen Frost Agentic Software. The wordmark is Aspen Frost; "agentic software" is the descriptor beneath it. We never use the word "empire" externally — that's an internal, tongue-in-cheek thing. Externally we are simply a software company that happens to be run a very new way.

2. Who It's For (right now)

The honest answer: the website's first audience is us, and the people Andy chooses to show it to. Aspen Frost is still closer to passion project than functioning business — but it is deliberately traveling that ramp. The site should make the trajectory legible without pretending we're already at the destination: serious enough to feel real, honest enough to admit the business is still becoming.

The ceiling, named honestly. Andy's best-case outcome is to run this and replace his income, plus a little — not to sell the company, raise money, or scale into something enterprise-shaped. That's the dream state, and it's the point. Everyone downstream (Zev, Izzy, Petra later) should stop optimizing for scale we don't want. The site is built to that ceiling, not past it.

Primary audience (now):

  • Andy — pride and proof. "Holy shit, we actually did this."
  • The team — the site makes the operation feel real and worth doing well.
  • The trusted few Andy shows it to — archetype: Erik & Amy. Erik (a clinician with a mental-health project idea) and Amy (a small-business owner). The reaction we're designing for: they start by giggling and end by thinking "wait — Andy could actually build this for us."
  • Friends-and-family project stakeholders who might commission real work. (Moved up from secondary in v1.4 — a deliberate near-term upgrade.) These are the people Andy would actually show this to and who could plausibly say "build mine." They belong in the primary circle now, not in a someday tier.

Secondary audience (later, not yet):

  • People who'd pay for the apps, or a small custom build. This is a future phase. We do not design the v1 site to chase external clients. We design it to be ready to point outward when the business is real.

NOT the audience — ever: enterprise buyers, eng leaders shopping dev shops, VC. This is not a "not yet." Given the income-replacement ceiling above, these are people we are explicitly not building for and not optimizing to impress. If one wanders in, fine — but we never write for them, and we never bend the brand to court them.

[needs-Petra / later] Real customer segmentation, buyer psychology, competitive alternatives, and willingness-to-pay are a later strategy phase. This brief intentionally does NOT assert them.

3. The Wedge

The one thing we can credibly claim that others can't.

It is not "we use AI agents." That is becoming table stakes. Anyone can spin up a few agents and get a pile of confident slop.

The wedge is the system. Andy is designing a structured operating system for agentic software — defined roles, adversarial review, a real build pipeline (design → architecture → build → deploy), handoffs, and a trust ramp. It's a system being actively built and proven, not a finished artifact: several working theories, tested against real projects and real work. That system is what turns "one guy and some agents" into actual shippable software. The proof is Frost Fitness.

The transparency is part of the wedge. Andy doesn't just run the system — he documents it in the open. Adversarial review, multiple models, how the handoffs actually work: the site shows the method, not just the output. Most shops hide how the sausage is made. We make the method the evidence. That openness is hard to copy and is itself a reason to believe.

Wedge, one line:

One person in a team of agents building real software you can actually use.

We never name the system publicly ("Empire OS" stays internal). We show the evidence of it and let the visitor conclude it's real.

4. The Three-Layer Slow Reveal

The site is built as a slow reveal. A first-time visitor experiences the work before they understand how it's made. The reveal has three layers, in this order:

  1. "Huh — interesting company." It reads like the website of a real software shop. Legitimate, confident, the work on display. We lead with company, not a single product. (Home / Projects)
  2. "Wait — it's one guy and a team of agents?" The double-take. (About)
  3. "...oh. It's not just one guy and some agents — it's one guy with a system that actually makes them work together." The mic drop. Roles, review, pipeline — the thing that makes the team function. (How We Work)

Layer 3 is where the credibility lives. Layers 1–2 earn the right to deliver it.

The agents speaking in their own distinct voices is the live demonstration of layer 3. If they all sounded the same, it's one person with a thesaurus. Different voices, distinct roles, visibly able to disagree — that's the system working in front of you. The voices are the evidence.

5. Voice & Register

Serious about the craft. Humble and open about the process. Genuinely enjoying the build.

  • Confident about the work, never boastful. We show receipts instead of adjectives.
  • Humble and open about the process — "humilosity." This is an experiment in public. "We've gotten good results, and we're still refining how we get them." The emotional core isn't humility alone — it's humility fused with curiosity: we don't assume we're right, and we're genuinely curious where we're wrong. That fusion has a name internally — humilosity — and it's strong enough to become a brand object on the site (see §7, Culture). This is fun, fascinating, and might genuinely turn out to be useful to people. We don't need to oversell it past that.
  • The delight is in the situation, never in the software. We never make jokes about the product. We let the premise (one person, a team of agents, a marketing fish) be charming on its own and play it straight. Restraint is the sophistication. Anyone can be cute about AI; almost nobody has the confidence to just show the real thing and let it land.
  • Plain sentence discipline. When the sentence has landed, stop. Avoid mirrored punchlines, rhythmic repetition, and clever second clauses that restate the point. Prefer the direct noun over the polished abstraction: "Frost Fitness" beats "a real product in real use." Cut soft qualifiers that sound like strategy-deck filler, especially when the sentence is stronger without them.

Anti-brand (what would make Andy close the tab):

  • Buzzword soup. Too many mentions of "AI." "Leverage," "synergy," "transformation," "revolutionize."
  • Trying too hard to be cute or clever.
  • Corporate fog — copy that sounds like it could belong to any company.
  • AI-ish rhetorical symmetry: "X, not Y" flourishes, repeated adjectives, and neat little echo phrases that sound written instead of said.
  • Overselling — claiming to be a company we're not yet, or dressing up the feeling past "this is fun and might be useful."

The read-aloud test applies to every line on this site. If you can't say it the way a human says it, it's not done.

Pronoun rule — "I" vs "we"

This is now a decision, not an open question.

"I" is the lead-in voice. Andy is the narrator when the site is talking about origin, ownership, intent, taste, or the plain fact of the company: I built this thing. This is my company. I am one person with a system of agents I built to work together. Hiding that behind corporate "we" buries the most interesting truth.

"We" is the system voice. Use "we" when the site is talking about what Aspen Frost does as an operation: culture, process, pipeline, projects, the agents, and the team at work on a build. In those moments, "we" is natural and unapologetic because it names the company as a working system, not a fake headcount.

Pattern: I made the system. We use the system. We build your project.

6. Message Hierarchy

Top to bottom, what a visitor should absorb in order:

  1. Hero / one true line. The idea to lodge: "Huh — maybe one guy and a team of agents really can build high-quality software." (Exact wording TBD; this is the idea we protect.)
  2. The work is real. Frost Fitness is live and in real use. Honest status labels on everything else.
  3. The team is real — and surprising. One person, a team of agents, each with a role and a voice.
  4. The system is real — and still being designed. How we work: a genuine pipeline with checks and balances, documented in the open, shown humbly.
  5. (Soft) you can follow along / reach out. No hard sell.

Locked line — Who We Are

At the top of the Who We Are section:

I've got your software team right here. I'm one person and a system of agents I built to work together. That's the whole company — and when we build your idea, it's the closest thing you'll get to a dev team of your own without hiring one.

This line is intentionally personal, slightly cheeky, and restrained. It makes the team feel present and yours without opening on the financial angle. The subhead models the pronoun rule: "I" for Andy as narrator; "we" when the team/system is doing the work.

The Humilosity device (Culture centerpiece)

Andy's note, and it's a good one: humilosity isn't a footnote, it's the heart. Treat it as a brand object — the word set as a dictionary entry, in a serif/dictionary font, with syllabic pronunciation. A reader who slows down for three seconds walks away with the whole philosophy in one glance.

hu·mil·os·i·ty  /(h)yo͞o-mi-ˈlä-sə-tē/  n. humility + curiosity, fused. We don't assume we're right; we'd rather find out where we're wrong. The operating belief of Aspen Frost.

It earns its keep three ways: it's ownable (you won't find it elsewhere), it's on-thesis (a made-up-but-true word for a made-up-but-real company), and it does the voice work in one object — serious about craft, humble and open about the process. Hand this to Izzy as a hero moment on Culture; let it echo on How We Work as the why behind adversarial review. (Final wording/pronunciation is a copy+design task.)

8. Call to Action

No hard sell. The impression is the conversion (for now).

  • Primary: "Follow." The journey is the story; watching an experiment become real is the hook. Caveat: placeholder only. We wire the button for later. We do NOT fake a feed/blog we won't maintain — a stale "latest post 4 months ago" actively damages the "this is real" thesis. The blog is an idea (Andy + Zev, someday), not a v1 commitment.
  • Secondary: "Contact." Quiet. A simple way for an Erik/Amy-type to start a conversation or submit an idea. Not prominent, not salesy.

If we can only ship one well, ship the impression and a working Contact; defer Follow until the blog is real.

8b. The Next-Rung CTA — "Cover the Tokens" (defined, NOT live on v1)

Andy's framing (2026-06-03), and it's sharper than a side-note — it's the CTA the rest of the brief was building toward. This is documented here as the next rung on the trust/stakes ramp. It does not go live on the v1 site.

The offer, in his words: "There are people who always wanted to hire a developer or a software firm, but it was never affordable. Now it is — especially in the early days. I'll build your idea, give you my time for free, and just ask you to cover the cost of the tokens."

Why it's strategically strong (not just generous):

  • It's a reframe nobody's heard: "custom software — you just cover the compute." It rewrites the cost structure of building software. That's a wedge in its own right.
  • It's honest — it maps exactly to where the operation is: still closer to passion project than functioning business, but deliberately traveling the ramp toward real revenue.
  • It's the perfect early-ramp step: low cost to both sides, real portfolio, real learning, real receipts — the thing that turns "huh, neat" into "wait, build mine."
  • It turns the site's quiet "Contact" into a genuine value proposition instead of a polite afterthought.

Why it is NOT on v1 — two gates, both above my pay grade:

  1. Stakes ramp. The moment this is public, you've invited real people with real expectations and (often) real data. EMPIRE-OS requires an explicit conversation before taking medium-stakes work. Turning this CTA on is a stakes-ramp decision (Andy + Aspen), not a copy decision (Zev).
  2. Capacity honesty. "I'll give my time for free" is a promise. With a day job, the realistic throughput is a couple of projects, not a faucet. An offer you can't fulfill damages the "this is real" thesis worse than no offer at all. When it goes live, it needs a built-in throttle — "taking on a couple of early projects" — not an open invitation.

The recommendation: design v1 so this CTA can be switched on the day Andy decides — the page structure, the Contact flow, and the messaging slot all exist and stay dormant. The site shows where we're going without overpromising where we are. When the stakes-ramp conversation happens, the copy is a short sprint, not a redesign.

[gate] Turning on the "cover the tokens" CTA = explicit Andy + Aspen stakes-ramp decision. Until then: documented direction, not live copy.

9. Aesthetic Direction (for Izzy — creative direction, not final design)

Anchors from Andy:

  • Background: ecru / warm paper, in the spirit of Anthropic/Claude's palette.
  • Accent: deep forest green where Claude uses orange. Green is the signature.
  • System accent: cyan / cold light as the agentic-system signal. The emerging palette logic is

hot + cold: Andy is human and warm; the agents are cold/systemic; Aspen Frost sits where those two meet. Forest green + cyan is the direction to keep exploring.

  • Motifs: trees, mountains, nature. Denver roots.
  • Soul: "Aspen" is a nature name and the name of Andy's late golden retriever. Warmth in the DNA. Not literal, not on a billboard — but it's why the brand feels warm rather than cold-tech.
  • Mark direction: updated mark is no longer the soft leaf direction. Current direction is a

six-pointed, slightly spooky, Frost Fitness-adjacent form with cyan and green. That is more on-thesis: still Aspen/Frost, but stranger, sharper, and closer to the software/agent system.

Direction: warm, calm, confident, natural. The opposite of neon-cyberpunk-AI. Trustworthy and human, with restraint. This is creative direction for Izzy to interpret, not a spec.

Design-system critique locked from Claude pass: the first pass was strategically close but visually too polite. It was too smooth, too polished, too tasteful-studio. Keep the honest structure, receipts, status labels, humilosity, and restrained confidence — but rough up the surface. The site needs more proof/artifact/system texture: real screenshots, logs, agent outputs, build traces, status labels that feel earned, and fewer generic card grids. It should not feel like a calm consultancy with nice typography. It should feel like Andy plus a working system.
